Procurement
First Principles

The foundational truths, operating model, core processes, and most common failure modes of the procurement function.

The Foundational Truths

These are the irreducible axioms of procurement — the principles that distinguish strategic procurement from transactional purchasing, and the ones most frequently violated when procurement underperforms.

Core Principles

P-01

Procurement exists to create value — not to process transactions

The moment procurement defines its purpose as "getting POs out," it has made itself a cost centre. The true mandate is converting external spend into business outcomes — lower cost, better quality, supply resilience, and supplier-driven innovation.

Measure procurement by value created, not transactions processed
P-02

Leverage is highest before commitment — and falls sharply after it

The moment a supplier knows they have the business, the dynamic shifts. Maximum leverage exists between market engagement and contract award. Everything after requires relationship management rather than market power.

Involve procurement at needs definition — not at purchase order stage
P-03

Spend visibility is the foundation of everything else

You cannot manage, segment, or strategise what you cannot see. Unclean or unclassified spend data means every decision is built on sand. Spend visibility is not a reporting project — it is operational infrastructure.

Spend data quality is a procurement accountability, not an IT problem
P-04

Supply markets are a strategic asset — understanding them is a competitive advantage

An organisation that understands its supply markets better than competitors — knows pricing dynamics, supplier capacity, new entrants, cost drivers — will consistently make better sourcing decisions.

Invest in market intelligence as you would in customer intelligence
P-05

Relationships reduce cost and risk in ways contracts cannot

A contract protects you when things go wrong. A strong relationship prevents things going wrong. Trusted suppliers prioritise your orders in shortages, bring innovations first, and resolve problems faster.

Segment suppliers by strategic value and invest relationship effort accordingly
P-06

Every procurement decision is simultaneously a cost and a risk decision

Cost and risk are always in tension. Lower price frequently means higher supply risk, lower quality, longer lead times, or weaker financial stability. Optimising for cost without accepting the corresponding risk is making an incomplete decision.

Document the risk position alongside every commercial decision
P-07

Compliance without value is bureaucracy

Policies and approval frameworks exist to create value. When the process produces friction without commensurate benefit, it incentivises workarounds. Bureaucracy is the fastest route to procurement irrelevance.

Audit your processes regularly — do they produce value, or just paperwork?
P-08

Procurement's influence is determined by when it gets involved

Early involvement means more options: demand shaping, specification challenge, market testing, sourcing model design. PO-stage involvement limits procurement to processing a decision already made by someone else.

Your internal reputation is a procurement asset — invest in it

The Central Tension

Cost vs. Risk vs. Value

Procurement sits at the intersection of three competing pressures: the business wants the lowest cost, the safest supply, and the most value. These frequently conflict — and the most important skill is not optimising any one, but consciously managing the trade-off between all three, with full stakeholder understanding of what is being accepted and what is being given up.

Savings Measurement — What Counts and What Doesn't

Hard Savings (Cash)

Reduces actual spend vs. prior year or approved budget. Directly verifiable in the P&L.

  • Price reduction on existing volume
  • Demand reduction / spec challenge
  • Competitive tender — lower price awarded
  • Supplier consolidation — eliminated duplication

Cost Avoidance

Savings against a proposed increase or market price. Real value — but does not reduce prior-year spend.

  • Negotiated against supplier price increase
  • Held price flat in inflationary market
  • Framework rate below spot market
  • Spec redesign that prevented cost increase

Soft / Value Benefits

Quantifiable improvements that don't directly reduce spend. Report separately — never conflate with hard savings.

  • Lead time improvement (reduces inventory)
  • Quality improvement (reduces COPQ)
  • Payment terms extension (improves WC)
  • Service level improvement

Not Savings — Common Inflation Tactics

Things reported as savings that are not. Finance sees through these.

  • Savings against an unrealistic budget
  • Re-reporting year-one saving in year two
  • Annualising partial-year saving without caveat
  • Claiming savings on off-contract spend

Common Failure Modes

Procurement fails in predictable patterns — some structural, some cultural, some analytical. These are the most common and costly failure modes across both operational and strategic procurement.

01

Procurement gets involved after the decision is already made

The business selects a preferred supplier informally and brings procurement in to "raise the PO." Procurement becomes a transaction processor. Leverage is zero.

Embed involvement triggers at budget approval or needs definition — not PO stage.
02

Maverick spend — the invisible leakage

In most organisations, 15–40% of spend bypasses procurement entirely. No commercial scrutiny, no volume leverage, compliance and financial risk.

Measure maverick spend as a headline KPI. Make preferred suppliers the path of least resistance.
03

Optimising price at the expense of total cost and risk

Awarding on lowest price is one of procurement's most destructive patterns. The cheapest supplier frequently has the highest total cost once quality, disruption, and overhead are included.

Use weighted evaluation criteria that reflect TCO. Price weight should reflect its actual importance — often 30–50%, not 100%.
04

Treating contract signature as the end of the job

A contract not actively managed becomes the floor, not the ceiling, of supplier performance. KPIs erode. The value captured in negotiation leaks away during the contract term.

Assign clear contract ownership. Define performance review cadence before signing.
05

Inflating savings to look good to finance

Savings against an unrealistic budget, re-reporting year-one savings, or claiming cost avoidance as hard savings. Finance eventually figures it out — and procurement loses the trust that enables strategic influence.

Agree a savings methodology with finance before reporting. Use separate lines for hard savings, cost avoidance, and value benefits.
06

Applying heavy process to low-value spend

Running a full RFP for a $5K category or requiring three quotes for routine items creates friction and destroys credibility for the categories where rigour actually matters.

Right-size the process to spend value and strategic importance. Use segmentation to allocate effort proportionally.
07

No coherent supplier base management

A bloated tail of hundreds of one-off suppliers creates overhead and loses leverage. But over-consolidation into single sources creates catastrophic supply risk.

Conduct a formal rationalisation exercise by category. Define the target supplier count and a tail spend elimination plan.
08

Procurement operating in isolation from the business

Strategies developed without stakeholder involvement die in implementation. Procurement's internal relationships are its most important asset — and the most neglected.

Embed procurement in the business through cross-functional teams. Make it easier to work with procurement than around it.
09

No spend data discipline

Dirty, incomplete, or poorly classified spend data means procurement cannot see what it manages. Every strategic decision is made partially blind.

Treat spend data quality as a programme. Assign ownership of spend classification. Audit the taxonomy quarterly.
10

Confusing activity with value — the busyness trap

Procurement can be extremely busy — processing POs, expediting — while creating very little strategic value. Busyness is not a proxy for performance.

Track the ratio of strategic to transactional activity. Automate routine P2P work. Protect time for sourcing strategy and market analysis.
Framework 02 · Category Management · Strategy · Reference

Category Management
First Principles

From foundational principles through process, key tools and models, and common failure modes of category strategy.

The Foundational Truths

First principles are the irreducible axioms of category management. Violate any one and your strategy will fail regardless of how sophisticated your tools or process are.

Core Principles

P-01

Categories exist to deliver business value — not procurement efficiency

A category is defined by the business need it enables, not how procurement wants to group spend. Your starting point is always: what does this spend make possible for the organisation?

Anchor every decision to a business outcome, not a savings target
P-02

Optimise for Total Cost of Ownership — never unit price

Price is the visible tip of the iceberg. TCO includes acquisition, quality failures, inventory carrying, transition costs, supply risk, and opportunity cost. Winning on price while losing on TCO is a common and expensive mistake.

Build a TCO model before any supplier engagement
P-03

Supply market intelligence is the foundation of strategy

You cannot develop a sound strategy without first understanding your supply market. Market concentration, supplier financials, input cost drivers, and capacity constraints all shape what is strategically possible.

No RFP before market analysis — ever
P-04

Segmentation determines the approach — not preference

The nature of the spend must dictate the sourcing model. A leverage category demands a different approach to a bottleneck category. Using the wrong model for a category type wastes effort or creates risk.

Apply Kraljic before choosing your sourcing strategy
P-05

Cross-functional alignment is not optional — it is the work

The technically best strategy that stakeholders haven't bought into is a strategy that will never be implemented. Stakeholder engagement is not a step in the process — it is continuous throughout.

No strategy without a RACI and stakeholder sign-off
P-06

Risk and value are equally weighted objectives

Category management is not a cost reduction programme. Organisations fail catastrophically by optimising purely for price at the cost of supply security. Risk-adjusted value is the correct metric.

Every category strategy needs an explicit risk position
P-07

Category management is a living cycle — not a one-time event

Markets change, demand shifts, suppliers evolve. A category plan that isn't reviewed is a historical artefact. The discipline is in treating the cycle as continuous, not treating contract signature as the finish line.

Schedule a formal category review at least annually

The Central Tension

Every category decision involves navigating a tension between value creation (lower TCO, better quality, innovation) and supply continuity (security, resilience, relationship depth). The Kraljic Matrix is ultimately a tool for resolving this tension by segment — which is why it underlies almost everything in the discipline.

Common Failure Modes

Category management fails in predictable ways. These are the most frequent and costly mistakes — and how to avoid them.

01

Jumping to RFP without market intelligence

Running a tender before understanding the supply market means you don't know who the real players are, what's competitive pricing, or how to structure the right commercial model. You're ceding negotiating power before you start.

Complete a supply market analysis (Porter's 5 Forces + supplier landscape) before designing any RFx.
02

Applying category management to every spend category

Category management is a high-effort discipline. Applying it to routine/non-critical spend is a misallocation of resource — the ROI isn't there.

Use Kraljic to segment first. Reserve full category management for strategic and leverage categories.
03

No stakeholder engagement until implementation

Developing a strategy in a procurement silo and presenting it for sign-off at the end guarantees resistance. Stakeholders who weren't part of the journey will not own the outcome.

Map stakeholders at Stage 1 and involve key players at every stage — especially strategy development.
04

Measuring savings on price, not TCO

Reporting "we saved 15% on unit price" while ignoring increased quality failures, higher inventory, or logistics cost is a vanity metric. It can actually mask a net loss to the business.

Build and track a TCO model. Savings should always be reported on a total cost basis.
05

Treating contract signature as the finish line

The value promised in a strategy is only realised through active implementation and ongoing performance management. Many category plans die at the contract stage because nobody owns what comes next.

Define Stage 7 KPIs, assign SRM ownership, and schedule QBRs before the contract is signed.
06

Ignoring the refresh cycle

A category strategy written two years ago and never reviewed is a historical document. Markets shift, demand changes, suppliers get acquired.

Define refresh trigger criteria (price movement threshold, demand shift %). Review formally at least annually.
07

Single-sourcing without explicit risk acceptance

Single-source strategies can be entirely justified — but must be a deliberate decision with documented risk acceptance, not the path of least resistance.

For any single-source decision, document the risk position, mitigation actions, and who has accepted the residual risk.
08

Over-specifying requirements

Specifications inherited from engineering are often over-engineered for the actual business need. Over-specification artificially restricts the supply market and increases cost without proportional value.

Challenge every specification at Stage 4. Ask: what business outcome does this specification enable?
Framework 03 · TCO Model · Costing · Reference

Total Cost of Ownership
First Principles

From foundational axioms of TCO through cost architecture, model-building process, and failure modes.

Why TCO Exists

TCO exists because price is a convenient lie. It is the one number that is always visible, always comparable, and almost never sufficient to make a good sourcing decision. TCO is the discipline of making the full cost of a decision visible before it is made — not after you live with the consequences.

Core Principles

P-01

Price is not cost — it is the smallest part of cost

In most sourcing decisions, purchase price accounts for 25–40% of true total cost. The remaining 60–75% lives in quality failures, logistics, inventory, transition, risk, and operational overhead — invisible until painful.

Never open a negotiation without a TCO model in hand
P-02

TCO is always comparative — it has no meaning in isolation

A TCO of $1.2M is meaningless. A TCO of $1.2M vs. $0.9M — where the difference is driven by quality risk — is decision-useful. TCO is a tool for comparing options against a common baseline.

Always model at least two scenarios; ideally three
P-03

Visibility creates accountability

When hidden costs are made explicit, they become someone's responsibility. A quality failure cost that was absorbed as "operations noise" becomes a procurement and supplier performance issue once it appears in the model.

Share the model with stakeholders — it changes the conversation
P-04

Risk must be quantified as cost — not described as a concern

"There is supply risk with this supplier" is an opinion. "Expected annual risk cost of $180K at 15% probability × $1.2M impact" is a business case. Unquantified risk is effectively ignored risk.

Assign probability and impact to every material risk — then put it in the model
P-05

Build the model before supplier engagement — not after

A TCO model built post-negotiation to justify a decision is a rationalisation exercise. It needs to be built before RFP issuance, because it defines what you are actually optimising for.

TCO model is an input to the RFP, not an output from it
P-06

The model informs the decision — it doesn't make it

A TCO model is only as good as its assumptions. Precision built on estimates creates false confidence. The model narrows the decision space and frames trade-offs — experienced judgment closes it.

Always document assumptions — and sensitivity-test the critical ones
P-07

The cost horizon must match the decision horizon

A one-year TCO for a five-year supplier relationship is a distortion. Onboarding, qualification, and transition costs only amortise over a longer period. Model over the realistic life of the decision.

Set the time horizon before building — typically 3–5 years for significant categories

Common Failure Modes

TCO modelling fails in predictable ways — ranging from structural errors in how the model is built to cultural failures in how it is used.

01

Modelling only the costs you can easily find

Modelling acquisition cost plus freight and calling it TCO captures maybe 50% of true cost — and misses exactly the costs that differentiate suppliers: quality, risk, and operational overhead.

Force yourself through every cost bucket in Tab 02. Either quantify each one or explicitly document why it is immaterial for this category.
02

Presenting estimates as facts

A model full of estimates presented without qualification creates false confidence. When assumptions are later challenged and found weak, the entire analysis loses credibility — including the parts that were correct.

Colour-code or flag every estimate. Maintain an assumption register. Run sensitivity analysis on estimates that matter most.
03

Ignoring transition costs when evaluating a switch

A new supplier at 15% lower TCO looks compelling until you add $180K in qualification costs, 6 months of dual-running, and $40K in tooling write-off. These real costs erode — and sometimes eliminate — the apparent saving.

Always include a transition bucket. Amortise one-off switching costs over the expected relationship duration before comparing annual TCO.
04

Describing risk instead of quantifying it

"This supplier carries significant geopolitical risk" is an observation. A TCO model requires a number: probability × impact = expected annual cost. Unquantified risk is effectively zero in a financial model.

Apply expected value formula to every material risk. If you cannot estimate probability and impact, use a conservative scenario and label it clearly.
05

Building the model after the decision is made

Reverse-engineering a TCO model to justify a preferred supplier is rationalisation, not analysis. It is detectable, intellectually dishonest, and exposes the organisation to a decision that was never properly evaluated.

Build the model before the RFP. The model's cost structure should directly inform evaluation criteria and their weighting.
06

Using a one-year time horizon for a multi-year decision

Qualification costs, tooling, and transition costs amortise over time. A supplier that looks expensive in year one may be significantly cheaper by year three. A one-year model makes the wrong decision look right.

Match time horizon to the realistic decision life. Use NPV for multi-year comparisons. Show the year-by-year curve, not just the aggregate.
07

Over-engineering the model at the expense of the decision

A 47-tab spreadsheet with 200 cost lines creates the illusion of rigour. The key insight — Option B is $220K cheaper over 3 years — is buried. Complexity is not credibility.

Keep the model at the level of granularity where additional detail changes the answer. If it doesn't change the answer, it doesn't belong in the model.
08

Never updating the model post-award

A model built, used, and then filed gives you 30% of its potential value. The real value is in comparing actual vs. modelled costs at 6, 12, and 24 months — which improves future assumptions and validates the decision.

Build an actual-vs-model tracking cadence into the category plan. Review TCO assumptions at the annual category review.

Negotiation

The structured pursuit of an agreement that meets your objectives — and holds under pressure.

First Principles

Before tactics and frameworks, these are the truths that govern every negotiation — regardless of category, counterpart, or commercial stakes.

01

Preparation is leverage

Whoever walks into the room knowing more — about their own position, the counterpart's constraints, and the market — holds structural advantage. Improvisation is not a strategy. The negotiation is largely won or lost before the first word is spoken.

02

BATNA determines your floor

Your Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement sets the minimum acceptable outcome. Without a credible BATNA, you cannot walk away — which means you cannot negotiate. Knowing your counterpart's BATNA tells you how hard you can push.

03

Interests, not positions

Positions are what parties say they want. Interests are why they want it. Two parties arguing over price may have entirely compatible underlying interests — delivery timing, volume certainty, cash flow. Negotiating interests opens far more solution space than trading positions.

04

The ZOPA defines what's possible

The Zone of Possible Agreement sits between your reservation price and theirs. If there is no ZOPA, no deal can be reached — and recognising this early saves both parties time. If the ZOPA is narrow, the negotiation is about who captures more of it.

05

Value is created before it is claimed

The best negotiations expand the pie before dividing it. Trading across multiple issues — price, volume, payment terms, lead time, tooling — allows value creation that pure price negotiation cannot achieve. Integrative negotiation outperforms distributive bargaining over the long run.

06

Silence is a tool

Most negotiators are uncomfortable with silence and fill it with concessions. After making a request or receiving a counter, silence signals that you are considering — not capitulating. Trained counterparts use silence deliberately.

07

Every concession sends a signal

How you concede matters as much as what you concede. Conceding quickly signals you had room to move. Conceding without receiving anything signals weakness. Each move is observed and interpreted. Concession strategy — speed, magnitude, and sequencing — must be planned in advance.

08

Relationship and outcome are both real costs

Winning a negotiation at the cost of a supplier relationship destroys value over time — especially in critical or sole-source categories. The goal is an agreement that both parties can execute with commitment, not a capitulation the supplier will find ways to recover from.

Preparation Framework

Systematic preparation across three dimensions: your own position, the counterpart's position, and the negotiable landscape between you.

Position Stack

Define three price/outcome anchors before any discussion begins. These are internal working documents — never disclosed.

Target Position

Opening Ask

The outcome you would be genuinely satisfied with. Ambitious but defensible — you should be able to justify it with data. This is where you open, or close to it. Many negotiators set targets too conservatively; anchor higher than comfortable.

Expected Position

Most Likely Outcome

The realistic settlement point based on market intelligence, prior deals, and counterpart constraints. Use this to calibrate whether a deal is on track or drifting. Do not anchor to this — it is a reference, not a target.

Reservation Price

Walk-Away Point

The absolute minimum acceptable outcome, set before the negotiation. Below this, your BATNA is preferable to the deal. This must be firm and pre-committed — deals that breach the reservation price are worse than no deal.

BATNA & ZOPA Analysis

Your BATNA

List every alternative to a deal: other qualified suppliers, redesign options, make-vs-buy, deferred purchase, existing inventory. Rank by value. Develop the best one far enough that it is genuinely credible — an untested BATNA is a bluff.

Improving your BATNA before entering a negotiation is the most direct way to improve your outcome — more effective than any in-room tactic.
Their BATNA

Research the counterpart's alternatives: capacity utilisation, other customers, order pipeline, financial health, strategic value of this account to them. A supplier operating at 60% utilisation has a weak BATNA. A sole-source with 18-month lead times does not.

Weakening their BATNA — by creating competitive alternatives or reducing your volume dependency — shifts the structural balance before a word is spoken.

Tactics & Execution

How to conduct yourself in the room — from opening moves to closing the deal. Tactics work best when deployed deliberately, not reactively.

Negotiation Styles

Collaborative (Integrative)+

Focus on shared interests and joint problem-solving. Best suited for strategic supplier relationships, long-term agreements, and complex multi-issue negotiations where value creation is possible. Requires trust and information sharing. The aim is an agreement both parties are committed to executing.

When to use: Preferred supplier negotiations, multi-year agreements, JDA/NDA situations, renewal of critical relationships.

Competitive (Distributive)+

Pure value-claiming in a fixed-sum context. Both parties know the ZOPA; the negotiation is about who captures more of it. Appropriate for transactional, commodity, or one-off purchases where the relationship is not the primary asset. Can damage trust if over-applied to strategic relationships.

When to use: Commodity auctions, spot purchases, one-off contracts with low strategic interdependence.

Principled (Fisher & Ury)+

Separate people from the problem. Focus on interests, not positions. Generate multiple options before deciding. Insist on objective criteria. The classic framework from Getting to Yes. Highly effective in complex or contentious negotiations where positional bargaining creates deadlock.

When to use: Disputes, contract renegotiations, high-stakes situations where maintaining the relationship through disagreement matters.

Tactical / Pressure-Based+

Anchoring, artificial deadlines, good cop/bad cop, limited authority plays. Effective short-term but requires awareness — sophisticated counterparts will recognise these moves and may escalate or disengage. Use selectively and consciously, not as a default.

When to use: Final-stage price compression on competitive bids, situations where the counterpart is using tactics first.

Concession Rules

Decreasing magnitude

Concede in progressively smaller increments: 5% → 3% → 1.5% → 0.5%. This signals you are approaching your limit. A consistent concession size (2%, 2%, 2%) invites the counterpart to keep pushing — they don't know where you stop.

Conditional giving

Every concession should be conditional: "If you can commit to 500 units per quarter, I can move on price." Unconditional concessions teach your counterpart that persistence alone generates movement — the worst possible signal.

Slow down as you close

Hesitation before a concession signals it is costly to make. Instant concessions suggest you had room all along. Pause, acknowledge the difficulty, then move — even if the concession was pre-planned.

Never concede last

The final concession defines the anchor for the next deal. If you always make the last move, you establish a pattern the counterpart will exploit. Aim for the final agreement to feel balanced — both sides gave.

Common Failure Modes

Negotiation failures are almost always preparation failures, emotional failures, or structural failures — rarely tactical ones.

01

Negotiating without a walk-away point

Entering a negotiation without a defined reservation price means any deal feels acceptable in the moment. Under pressure, without a pre-committed floor, people accept outcomes they will regret. The reservation price must be set before the conversation starts — not calculated during it.

Set your BATNA and reservation price before entering. Write them down. Have them physically in front of you during the negotiation.
02

Opening too close to your target

A conservative opening signals you are already near your limit. It destroys anchoring power and removes room to make meaningful concessions — which the counterpart will interpret as inflexibility. Open ambitiously but defensibly.

Your opening position should be higher than your target by enough to allow 2–3 meaningful moves. If you can't justify the opening with data, you've gone too far.
03

Talking too much

The instinct to fill silence, justify every position, and over-explain is universal — and counterproductive. Every additional word is information. Every justification invites a rebuttal. Say your number, stop, and wait.

Make a proposal in one sentence. Then stop. Silence is not a problem to solve — let the counterpart respond.
04

Treating negotiation as confrontation

Adversarial framing creates resistance and shuts down information sharing. In procurement, you will negotiate with this supplier again — possibly next month. A win that damages the relationship has a long tail of cost: lower priority, less transparency, reluctance to solve problems.

Frame the conversation as joint problem-solving. Use "we" and "us" language. Signal that you want them to succeed too, within the constraints of a fair deal.
05

Single-issue negotiating on price alone

When the only variable is price, the negotiation becomes pure zero-sum. A skilled counterpart will hold firm. Multi-issue negotiations create trade opportunities — and often deliver more total value than a 5% price reduction.

Build your issues map before entering. Identify at least 4–6 negotiable variables. Introduce them early — not as a surprise at the end.
06

Accepting the first counter without testing

A counterpart who accepts your opening immediately without negotiating has either given you a genuine concession — or your opening was too generous. The absence of pushback is data: you may have left value on the table.

Even when a first counter looks acceptable, make at least one further move. Test whether the position is truly firm before closing.
07

Confusing being liked with being effective

The desire to be seen as reasonable, flexible, and easy to work with drives premature concessions. Effective negotiators are consistently fair — not consistently accommodating. The goal is a good outcome, not a good impression.

Separate personal likeability from negotiation effectiveness. Firm positions held respectfully are not confrontational. Prepare responses to discomfort in advance.
08

No post-negotiation debrief

Most organisations close a deal and immediately move on. The intelligence gathered — what worked, what didn't, what the counterpart revealed about their constraints — is lost. This makes every subsequent negotiation with the same party a cold start.

Debrief within 48 hours. Document counterpart behaviour, revealed interests, effective moves, and what you wish you'd done differently. Build a negotiation file per supplier.

Contract Management

The discipline of converting a signed agreement into sustained commercial value — and protecting against the erosion that begins the moment ink dries.

First Principles

Contract management is where procurement value is protected or destroyed. Most sourcing effort is wasted if post-award governance is weak.

01

Value leaks post-award, not pre-award

Research consistently shows 20–40% of negotiated savings evaporate within 24 months of contract signature through price creep, scope changes, non-compliance, and missed performance triggers. The negotiation is the start of value capture — not the end.

02

A contract is a management tool, not a filing event

The signed document has no value on a shelf. Its value lies in active use: referencing obligations, tracking deliverables, enforcing remedies, and capturing variations formally. Contracts that aren't read aren't managed.

03

Clarity at signature prevents disputes later

Ambiguous performance measures, undefined acceptance criteria, and vague scope descriptions create interpretation risk. What seems obvious at signing becomes contentious at year two. Precision in drafting is an investment in dispute avoidance.

04

Obligations run both ways

Contract management is not just about holding the supplier accountable. Buyer obligations — purchase volume commitments, forecast provision, timely payment, information sharing — must be tracked equally. Buyer non-performance is the supplier's most common defence.

05

Scorecards create accountability without confrontation

Regular, data-driven performance reviews depersonalise difficult conversations. A supplier consistently scoring 68/100 on delivery has a documented problem — not an accusation. Objective measurement is the foundation of both improvement and escalation.

06

Changes are inevitable — the process is what matters

No contract survives contact with reality unchanged. The question is whether variations are managed formally (documented, priced, and agreed) or informally (verbal, undocumented, and disputed). Informal change management is the fastest way to lose contractual protection.

07

Exit planning starts at signature

Transition-out terms, data portability, knowledge transfer obligations, and exit assistance clauses must be negotiated before you need them — not when the relationship is already deteriorating. Suppliers have little incentive to facilitate smooth exits without contractual obligation.

Contract Architecture

The six structural domains every well-constructed commercial contract must address. Weakness in any one creates exposure.

Commercial Terms+

Pricing structure: Fixed, variable, cost-plus, indexed, or hybrid. Define the base clearly. If indexed, specify the index, the floor/ceiling, the review cadence, and whether it moves symmetrically (up and down) or only upward.

Payment terms: Payment timing, currency, dispute escalation process for contested invoices, early payment discount terms if applicable.

Volume commitments: Minimum purchase obligations, forecast obligations, and consequences of shortfall. Asymmetric terms (you commit to minimums, they don't commit to supply) are a common trap.

Benchmarking rights: The right to periodically compare contract pricing against market rates, with a process to negotiate adjustment if a defined threshold is exceeded.

Performance Framework+

KPIs and SLAs: Must be specific, measurable, and tied to outcomes that matter (not just activity). Define measurement methodology, data sources, and who calculates. Ambiguous KPIs are unenforceable KPIs.

Remedies and incentives: Penalties (service credits, price reductions) for underperformance and bonuses for outperformance. The mechanism must be defined — not just the trigger. Who claims? By when? What is the limit?

Review cadence: Monthly operational reviews, quarterly performance reviews, annual strategic reviews. Each level has different participants, outputs, and escalation thresholds.

Continuous improvement: Annual improvement targets — typically 2–5% cost reduction or quality/efficiency improvement — prevent the contract from becoming a price floor that drifts upward with indexation.

Risk & Liability+

Liability caps: Most commercial contracts limit total liability to a multiple of contract value (1–2× annual fees is common). Understand what is excluded from the cap — typically fraud, gross negligence, IP infringement, and death/injury.

Indemnities: Who indemnifies whom for what. IP infringement indemnities from the supplier are critical in technology and component supply. Product liability indemnities matter in OEM supply chains.

Insurance requirements: Minimum coverage types and limits, named insured obligations, requirement to notify on material policy changes. Request certificates annually.

Force majeure: Defines what events suspend obligations. Review carefully — overbroad force majeure clauses allow suppliers to excuse non-performance for events well within their control.

Change Management+

Variation process: All changes to scope, specification, timeline, or price must follow a defined process: written request → impact assessment → pricing proposal → written approval. No verbal changes. No retrospective variations.

Escalation path: Who is authorised to approve changes at what value threshold. Changes above threshold require escalation to defined governance level.

Change log: A running register of all approved variations, cumulative impact on price and scope, and authorising parties. Maintained by both parties. Reviewed at each performance review.

Termination & Exit+

Termination for convenience: The right to exit without cause, with defined notice period. Critical for maintaining optionality. Some suppliers resist this — it is worth fighting for, especially in longer-term contracts.

Termination for cause: Specific trigger events (persistent KPI failure, insolvency, material breach) that allow immediate or accelerated exit. The trigger events must be precisely defined.

Transition assistance: The exiting supplier's obligation to support transition to a replacement: knowledge transfer, data export, parallel running, staff introductions. Must be scoped and priced before signing — not negotiated during a fractious exit.

Step-in rights: In critical supply relationships, the right to assume direct operational control (or appoint a third party to do so) if the supplier fails to perform and the failure is creating material risk.

Governance & Relationship+

Relationship owners: Named individuals on both sides with defined authority and contact protocols. Prevents the relationship from existing only between individuals who may leave.

Meeting cadence and outputs: Each review tier produces a defined output: action log, performance scorecard, escalation items, approved changes. Minutes are circulated and agreed within a defined timeframe.

Dispute resolution: Escalation ladder before litigation or arbitration: operational → management → executive → formal dispute. Defines timeframes at each stage. Prevents disputes from escalating prematurely.

Records and audit: The supplier's obligation to maintain records supporting invoiced amounts, and your right to audit them. Scope, frequency, and notice requirements. Cost allocation for audit.

Common Failure Modes

Contract management failures are mostly governance failures and attention failures — not legal failures.

01

Filing the contract and never returning to it

The most common failure. The contract is signed, celebrated, and shelved. No one tracks KPIs, obligations or review dates. Value leaks immediately and invisibly. A contract that isn't actively used is worth nothing.

Mandatory contract register for all spend above threshold. Calendar every key date. Assign a named contract owner with explicit accountability.
02

Missing the renewal window

Auto-renewal clauses activate because no one set a reminder. The organisation then spends the next 2–3 years locked in at terms negotiated years ago, without the leverage of a pending decision. Some suppliers engineer contracts specifically to benefit from this.

Set renewal assessment alerts 12 months before expiry. For contracts over a defined value, require a written renewal decision — including the option to retender — before the notice period closes.
03

Allowing verbal changes to accumulate

A project manager says "yes" to a scope addition verbally. Six months later the supplier invoices for it and has an email trail. The buyer disputes it and loses — because informal agreement was made without authority or process.

Blanket policy: no scope, price, or timeline changes without a written variation agreement. Train all stakeholders who interact with suppliers on this — not just procurement.
04

KPIs that measure activity, not outcomes

"On-time delivery: 95%" sounds robust until you discover that "on-time" was defined as despatch, not receipt — and that it excludes the 12% of lines that were rescheduled before delivery. Poorly defined KPIs are gamed without technically being breached.

Define measurement methodology precisely in the contract. Include data source, calculation formula, and what is excluded. Test the KPI calculation against real data before finalising wording.
05

Never invoking remedies when they're triggered

Performance drops below the penalty threshold, but the relationship manager doesn't want to raise it. The credit is never claimed. The supplier learns that the penalties have no teeth. The next breach is larger.

Invoking a remedy as contracted is not aggressive — it is the mechanism the contract was designed around. Establish clear internal authority for when and how to invoke remedies, so it doesn't require a difficult personal decision.
06

Handover failure from sourcing to operations

The sourcing team negotiates a sophisticated agreement with tiered pricing, complex KPIs, and specific invoicing requirements. The operations and finance teams who execute it daily have never read it. Three months in, the pricing is being applied incorrectly and no one knows.

Mandatory contract handover session for all operational stakeholders. Written summary of key obligations, pricing rules, and escalation paths. Finance teams must understand the invoicing requirements before the first invoice arrives.
07

No exit clause — or an unusable one

A contract without termination for convenience locks you in regardless of performance, market change, or strategic shift. Termination for cause clauses with impossibly high thresholds ("persistent material breach over 12 consecutive months") offer little real protection.

Fight for termination for convenience in every significant contract. Ensure termination for cause triggers are precise and proportionate. Model the exit cost before signing — not after the relationship deteriorates.
08

Treating contract management as purely administrative

When contract management sits in a support role with no commercial authority, decisions default to the relationship manager — who is focused on operational outcomes, not commercial protection. Value leaks when commercial and operational accountability are separated.

Assign commercial responsibility for contract performance to a named person with authority to invoke terms. This may be a procurement business partner, category manager, or senior operations manager — but it must be explicit.

Supplier Risk Management

The systematic identification, assessment, and mitigation of risks that originate in the supply base — before they materialise as operational failures.

First Principles

Supply risk is not a compliance exercise. It is a strategic function that determines whether your supply chain can execute when conditions are unfavourable — which they will be.

01

Risk that isn't measured isn't managed

Describing risk qualitatively ("this supplier carries concentration risk") creates awareness but not action. Risk management requires quantification: probability, impact, and expected value. Without numbers, there is no basis to prioritise mitigation spend or compare options.

02

Not all suppliers warrant the same scrutiny

Apply risk management effort proportionally. Disruption to a critical sole-source component has asymmetric consequences compared to a disruption in a commodity category with 12 qualified alternatives. Tier your supplier base before you tier your risk monitoring.

03

Risk is dynamic, not static

A supplier's risk profile changes constantly — financially, geopolitically, operationally. A supplier who was green on every metric 18 months ago may be in financial distress today. Risk assessments must be living documents, refreshed on a cadence proportional to criticality.

04

Concentration is a risk category in itself

Over-dependency on a single supplier, single geography, single transport route, or single technology platform creates fragility regardless of how well that supplier performs today. Concentration risk is structural — it cannot be mitigated by better supplier management alone.

05

Mitigation has a cost — and so does residual risk

Dual sourcing, safety stock, geographic diversification, and alternative qualification all cost money. The mitigation investment must be calibrated against the expected cost of the risk it protects against. Overspending on mitigation is as poor a decision as under-managing risk.

06

Early warning is worth more than incident response

A disruption that is anticipated 90 days in advance has a fraction of the operational and commercial impact of one that arrives without warning. The value of a risk monitoring programme lies primarily in advance notice — not in post-event analysis.

07

Sub-tier risk is real and often invisible

Tier 1 suppliers may appear robust while their own supply chains carry material concentration risk. A single-source sub-component three tiers deep can disable an otherwise diverse supply chain. Understanding sub-tier exposure — even at a high level — is a strategic requirement for critical categories.

08

Risk management and supplier development are complementary

Many supply risks are best mitigated by investing in supplier capability — financial support, technical assistance, demand visibility sharing — rather than by building alternatives. Treating risk as purely adversarial misses the development pathway that often delivers better outcomes at lower cost.

Risk Taxonomy

Eight categories of supply risk, each with distinct drivers, early warning signals, and mitigation approaches.

Supply Disruption Risk+

Definition: Risk of interruption to the physical flow of goods — from any cause that prevents delivery to schedule.

Drivers: Capacity constraints, natural disaster, logistics failure, factory incident, labour dispute, raw material shortage.

Early warnings: Extended lead time creep, increased expediting requests, sub-tier shortage reports, logistics capacity tightening.

Mitigations: Safety stock, dual sourcing, alternative logistics routing, demand-supply visibility sharing, supplier capacity reservation agreements.

Quality Risk+

Definition: Risk that supplied goods fail to meet specification, leading to returns, rework, production stops, warranty claims, or field failures.

Drivers: Process control failures, incoming material variation, workforce turnover, capacity pressure driving shortcuts, inadequate change management.

Early warnings: Rising incoming inspection failure rates, increased supplier corrective actions (SCARs), customer field return rates, supplier quality audit findings.

Mitigations: Incoming inspection protocols, supplier quality audits, first article inspection requirements, statistical process control requirements, supplier development programmes.

Financial Risk+

Definition: Risk of supplier financial distress — reduced capability, erratic behaviour, or outright failure — that impairs their ability to perform.

Drivers: Customer concentration, over-leverage, thin margins, payment disputes, loss of major customer, rising input costs, credit tightening.

Early warnings: Requests for extended payment terms or early payment, credit rating downgrades, public financial reports showing deteriorating ratios, longer quotation validity, reduced investment in plant or people.

Mitigations: Annual financial health review using public accounts or D&B reports, supply chain finance programmes for critical suppliers, dual sourcing for financially fragile sole sources, escrow of critical tooling, IP, and documentation.

Geopolitical & Trade Risk+

Definition: Risk arising from political events, trade policy changes, sanctions, tariffs, or regulatory changes in supplier geographies.

Drivers: Export controls, tariff escalation, sanctions regimes, political instability, conflict, trade bloc changes, currency controls.

Early warnings: Policy announcements, diplomatic deterioration, trade negotiation breakdowns, supplier requests for price adjustments citing "regulatory changes."

Mitigations: Geographic diversification of supply base, tariff classification review, free trade agreement utilisation, country-of-origin monitoring, alternate qualification in lower-risk geographies.

Regulatory & Compliance Risk+

Definition: Risk that the supplier fails to meet applicable regulations — environmental, safety, chemical content, labour — creating legal exposure or import restrictions for the buyer.

Drivers: Regulatory change, inadequate compliance management at the supplier, sub-tier non-compliance (e.g. conflict minerals, RoHS substances), evolving international standards.

Early warnings: Supplier non-responsiveness to compliance questionnaires, failure to provide updated certifications, changes in product composition without notification.

Mitigations: Annual compliance declarations, third-party audits, inclusion of regulatory compliance obligations in contracts, sub-tier material disclosure requirements (e.g. IMDS, IPC-1752).

Cyber & Data Security Risk+

Definition: Risk arising from the supplier's cybersecurity posture — including the possibility that a breach at the supplier creates exposure for your systems, IP, or data.

Drivers: Shared system access, EDI/API integrations, design file sharing, supplier hosting of your data, supplier staff with access to your systems.

Early warnings: News of supplier cyber incidents, failure to meet security questionnaire standards, supplier requests for elevated system access.

Mitigations: Supplier security questionnaire (aligned to ISO 27001 or NIST), contractual security obligations, penetration testing requirements, limitation of data shared, incident notification clauses with defined timelines.

ESG & Reputational Risk+

Definition: Risk that supplier practices on environmental, social, or governance dimensions create reputational damage, regulatory exposure, or loss of customer confidence for your organisation.

Drivers: Modern slavery, unsafe labour practices, environmental violations, corruption, governance failures — in the supplier or their sub-tier.

Early warnings: NGO reports, media coverage, supplier audit non-conformances, modern slavery statement omissions, environmental incident disclosures.

Mitigations: Supplier code of conduct, self-assessment questionnaires, third-party audits, contractual right to audit for compliance, traceability programmes for high-risk material categories.

Concentration Risk+

Definition: Structural over-dependency on a single supplier, geography, technology, or supply route — creating disproportionate exposure to any disruption within that node.

Drivers: Historical single-sourcing, technology lock-in, high qualification costs, market monopoly/duopoly, geographic clustering of supply.

Early warnings: Category reviews that reveal high sole-source percentages, supplier commercial behaviour suggesting awareness of leverage (slower response, less competitive pricing), geographic supply concentration increasing.

Mitigations: Dual/multi-source strategy, alternative qualification programmes, design-for-substitution at product level, volume rebalancing, strategic buffer stock for sole-source critical items.

A six-stage cycle that converts supply risk from an unmanaged background threat into a monitored, prioritised, and mitigated set of known exposures.

Conduct sub-tier mapping for the most critical items — understand where your Tier 1 supplier sources their key inputs. Include risks across the full supply chain: raw materials, manufacturing, logistics, regulatory, and financial.

Dual sourcing, geographic diversification, safety stock, alternative qualification, long-term agreements for critical supply, sub-tier development — these change the risk architecture permanently.

Supply continuity clauses, business continuity requirements, material escrow, source code escrow, tooling ownership, force majeure scope limits — these create legal leverage if disruption occurs.

Common Failure Modes

Supply risk failures almost always trace back to structural blind spots, monitoring gaps, or the organisational failure to act on known risks in advance.

01

Risk registers that are created once and never updated

A risk register built during a sourcing exercise and then filed is not risk management — it is risk documentation. Supply risk changes constantly. A static register gives false assurance while actual exposures evolve.

Embed risk register refresh into the annual category review cycle. Trigger off-cycle updates when significant events occur: supplier financial results, geopolitical events, quality incidents, ownership changes.
02

Confusing supplier performance with supplier risk

A supplier delivering on time, on quality, and on cost today may carry high latent risk: financial fragility, geographic concentration, sub-tier exposure, or regulatory non-compliance. Current performance is not a proxy for risk profile.

Maintain separate scorecards for performance and risk. A supplier can be green on delivery and red on financial health simultaneously. Both require management attention.
03

Ignoring sub-tier risk

Most supply chain disruptions do not originate at Tier 1. They originate two or three tiers deep — in a sub-supplier that neither you nor your direct supplier has mapped. Sole-source sub-components, geographic clustering of sub-tier suppliers, and shared raw material dependencies create invisible concentration risk.

For critical and strategic categories, require Tier 1 suppliers to disclose key sub-tier dependencies. Map at least to Tier 2 for sole-source critical components. Include sub-tier risk assessment in sourcing decisions.
04

Building safety stock as a substitute for risk management

Safety stock addresses one risk category (supply disruption) for one dimension (timing) and creates its own costs: capital, storage, obsolescence. It does not address quality, financial, geopolitical, or regulatory risks. And it is finite — a prolonged disruption exhausts it regardless.

Treat safety stock as one mitigation tool among many — appropriate for specific risk profiles. Do not use it as an excuse to avoid structural mitigations (dual sourcing, alternative qualification) that require more effort but deliver more durable protection.
05

Knowing about a risk and accepting it without documentation

"We've always known that supplier is financially fragile" is not risk management. Undocumented accepted risks exist in the minds of individuals who may leave, be overruled, or simply forget. When the event occurs, the organisation has no record of having assessed it.

Every material risk that is accepted rather than mitigated must be documented: risk description, probability and impact, rationale for acceptance, and the name of the person accepting it. Document at the appropriate governance level for the risk magnitude.
06

No contingency plans — or plans that only exist on paper

A risk register with mitigation plans that have never been tested or even discussed with operations is not a contingency plan — it is a document. Untested plans fail under pressure because the team has not internalised them and the logistics have not been validated.

Tabletop exercise the most critical risk scenarios annually. Validate that alternative suppliers can actually deliver at the required volumes and specifications. Test the response sequence before you need it.
07

Risk management as a procurement-only activity

Supply risk events impact operations, finance, sales, and customers — but risk management is often conducted by procurement in isolation. When an event occurs, the rest of the organisation is unprepared and the response is uncoordinated.

Establish cross-functional risk governance for material supply risks. Engineering, operations, finance, and senior management need visibility of the top risks and ownership of elements of the response plan. Risk briefings should reach executive level for critical supplier exposures.
08

Treating risk as a sourcing-phase activity only

Risk assessed during RFP evaluation is a point-in-time view. Supply risk evolves throughout the relationship lifetime. Concentrating risk management effort at the sourcing phase while ignoring it in steady-state operations is the same as insuring a building at construction and never renewing the policy.

Integrate risk monitoring into ongoing supplier management. Include risk metrics in supplier scorecards. Make risk status a standing agenda item at quarterly supplier reviews for critical and strategic suppliers.

Spend Analysis

The structured interrogation of what your organisation buys, from whom, at what cost — and what that data reveals about opportunity, risk, and leverage.

First Principles

Spend analysis is the foundational intelligence layer of procurement. Without it, every sourcing decision, category strategy, and supplier discussion is conducted with incomplete information.

01

You cannot manage what you cannot see

Procurement can only exert leverage over spend it knows about. Unanalysed spend is effectively unmanaged — pricing is unchallenged, supplier relationships are unmonitored, and opportunities remain invisible. Visibility precedes all other value creation.

02

Data quality is the binding constraint

Spend analysis is only as good as the underlying data. Miscoded transactions, inconsistent supplier naming, missing cost centres, and poor classification render analysis unreliable — and decisions made on it are worse than no decision. Data cleansing and classification are not optional pre-work; they are the work.

03

Classification determines insight

Raw transaction data grouped incorrectly tells a false story. The way spend is classified — by category, commodity, supplier, geography, cost centre, and business unit — determines what questions you can answer. Your classification taxonomy must be designed with the analysis questions in mind.

04

Supplier rationalisation is usually the first opportunity

Most organisations have far more active suppliers than they need. Long tails of low-volume, low-value suppliers consume disproportionate management time, create compliance risk, and dilute the leverage that could be concentrated with a smaller, better-managed supply base. The tail is consistently the first place spend analysis finds savings.

05

Spend analysis is a recurring process, not a project

A one-time spend analysis is a snapshot. The value of spend analysis compounds when it is refreshed regularly and compared over time — revealing shifts in supplier dependency, category growth, maverick spend patterns, and pricing drift that point-in-time analysis cannot show.

06

The goal is insight, not taxonomy

Organisations can spend months perfecting a commodity classification hierarchy and produce a beautifully organised dataset that generates no commercial decisions. Classification is a means to an end: actionable insight that changes what procurement does. Every classification decision should be tested against the question — does this enable better decisions?

07

Maverick spend reveals process failure

Off-contract, off-policy purchases do not just represent missed savings — they signal that the procurement process, catalogue, or contracted supplier is not meeting the need. Maverick spend is a diagnostic signal, not just a compliance problem. Understanding why it happens points to the structural fix.

The Spend Cube

Spend data has three primary dimensions. Slicing across them in combination reveals patterns that single-dimension analysis misses.

Dimension 1

What — Category / Commodity

What is being purchased. Structured in a hierarchy: Category → Sub-category → Commodity code. A well-designed taxonomy allows roll-up and drill-down. Aligns with UNSPSC, CPV, or a custom internal coding structure.

Dimension 2

Who — Supplier

From whom. Requires supplier normalisation: all variations of a legal entity name resolved to a single canonical supplier record. Parent-child relationships mapped so group spend is visible. Enables concentration analysis and tail identification.

Dimension 3

Where — Business Unit / Cost Centre

Who inside the organisation is buying. Enables cross-business-unit aggregation (combining fragmented spend across BUs to build leverage), compliance monitoring by cost centre, and internal stakeholder conversations grounded in data rather than perception.

Common Failure Modes

Spend analysis failures are mostly data discipline failures and scope failures — the analysis is either built on bad data or stopped before it generates commercial action.

01

Analysing only PO-backed spend

PO-backed spend is the easiest data to pull but often represents only 60–75% of actual expenditure. The remaining 25–40% — P-card, direct invoices, corporate card — is disproportionately likely to be maverick, uncontracted, and overpriced. Analysing only clean data produces a systematically optimistic picture.

Always anchor spend analysis on the AP ledger — all paid invoices — and reconcile to the PO data. The gap between the two is the first thing to investigate.
02

Treating supplier name normalisation as optional

Leaving "ACME Corp", "ACME Corporation NZ", "Acme Supplies" as three separate entities understates concentration, inflates supplier count, and produces meaningless Pareto analysis. This is not a data quality nicety — it is the prerequisite for every supplier-level insight the analysis is meant to generate.

Build a supplier normalisation map before running any analysis. This is typically 30–50% of the total effort for a first spend analysis — and worth every minute.
03

Over-classifying at the expense of completeness

Designing a 10-level commodity hierarchy that requires 95% manual classification is a path to a 40% unclassified residual and a project that takes six months and still isn't finished. Complexity that cannot be sustained operationally produces nothing.

Design the taxonomy at the level of granularity that drives different strategic decisions. Three to five levels is almost always sufficient. Automate what you can. Accept that 90% classified on time is better than 97% classified never.
04

Running analysis once and not repeating it

A spend analysis conducted 18 months ago reflects a business that no longer exists. Procurement decisions based on stale data miss new opportunities, overstate saved opportunities that have already been captured, and miss new concentration or compliance risks that have emerged.

Establish a quarterly or semi-annual refresh cycle. The first analysis is the hardest — subsequent iterations are incremental updates. Use the same taxonomy and normalisation rules to enable trend comparison.
05

Presenting analysis without prioritised actions

A 40-page spend analysis report with 12 charts and no prioritised action list creates admiration, not results. Stakeholders walk away impressed and unchanged. The value of the analysis is entirely in the decisions it enables.

Every presentation of spend analysis findings should end with a prioritised opportunity list: name, estimated value, owner, and timeline. The analysis exists to fund the pipeline — not to be the deliverable.
06

Ignoring the tail because it seems small

The supplier tail — the bottom 80% of suppliers by spend, representing perhaps 20% of total spend — is dismissed as immaterial. But this is typically hundreds of suppliers, many uncontracted, many duplicating capabilities of contracted providers, and collectively consuming significant administrative resource.

Tail rationalisation — consolidating or eliminating low-value suppliers — typically delivers 3–8% direct savings plus disproportionately large administrative efficiency gains. It is often the fastest win in a spend analysis programme.
07

Using spend analysis to confirm existing beliefs

The most common bias in spend analysis is building the taxonomy and analysis approach around the categories you already manage, which produces data that confirms procurement is managing the right things — and systematically misses the categories where it isn't present at all.

Include a coverage map in every spend analysis: categories with active strategies vs. total spend by category. The whitespace — categories with significant spend and no strategy — is often the most important finding.

Should-Cost Modelling

The independent estimation of what a product or service ought to cost to produce — built from engineering and market data, not supplier quotations.

First Principles

Should-cost modelling shifts the sourcing conversation from "what does the supplier charge?" to "what should it cost to make?" — a fundamentally different posture.

01

Cost is structured, not opaque

Every product or service cost can be decomposed into identifiable elements: materials, labour, overhead, tooling, logistics, and margin. None of these is inherently unknowable. A supplier's refusal to explain their cost structure is a negotiating posture, not an economic necessity.

02

The model is independent of the supplier's quote

A should-cost model is built from publicly available data — commodity indices, regional labour rates, industry overhead benchmarks, freight tariffs — without reference to what any specific supplier has proposed. This independence is its value. When the model and the quote diverge significantly, that gap demands an explanation.

03

It establishes your negotiation floor, not your target price

The should-cost figure is not the price you demand. It is the minimum below which the supplier cannot sustainably operate. Your target price sits above it — ideally at a level that leaves the supplier with fair margin, while ensuring you are not subsidising their inefficiency.

04

Preparation, not confrontation

Sharing a well-constructed should-cost analysis with a supplier signals that you are technically informed, market-aware, and prepared. It changes the dynamic from "you must justify your price" to "here is our understanding — where does it differ from yours and why?" Sophisticated suppliers respect this. It accelerates productive conversation.

05

It is most powerful before the RFQ, not after

A should-cost model built before issuing an RFQ shapes the specification, the evaluation criteria, and the target price range. A model built after responses are received is reactive — useful, but less strategically powerful. The timing of the model determines how much of its value you can capture.

06

Engineering knowledge is a prerequisite

Should-cost modelling is most effective when procurement works alongside engineering and manufacturing to understand the process steps, material quantities, and cycle times involved. Without technical input, the model relies on approximations that a supplier can easily challenge. Cross-functional collaboration is not optional.

07

Commodity market data is live intelligence

Direct material costs — metals, polymers, rare earths, PCB substrates — are driven by commodity markets that move independently of any supplier relationship. Tracking these indices gives you early warning of price movements and a factual basis to challenge increases or demand reductions.

08

The gap between should-cost and quoted price is a data point

A supplier quoting 40% above your should-cost model is not necessarily wrong — they may have constraints, capabilities, or risks your model doesn't capture. But that gap must be explained and justified. Unexplained gaps represent either supplier margin opportunity or model gaps that need investigation — both are valuable.

Cost Architecture

Every manufactured product or service cost decomposes into six primary buckets. Understanding each element — its drivers, variability, and benchmarks — is the foundation of an accurate model.

Direct Materials+

The largest cost element in most manufactured products. Build from the Bill of Materials (BOM): list every component and raw material, source current market pricing (spot or contract), apply a yield/scrap factor, and account for minimum order quantities that may require carrying inventory.

Key data sources: London Metal Exchange (LME) for base metals, ICIS for polymers and chemicals, component distributors (Digi-Key, Mouser, Arrow) for electronic parts, IHS Markit / Omdia for semiconductor pricing trends.

Watch for: Commodity substitutions the supplier may be making without your knowledge, obsolescence risk in long-lead components, dual-use materials subject to export control.

Direct Labour+

Labour cost = (process time in hours) × (fully-loaded labour rate). Process time is derived from engineering estimates, time studies, or analogy to similar products. Rates vary significantly by geography — a PCB assembly in Shenzhen costs a fraction of the same assembly in Germany.

Key data sources: ILO wage databases, Aon/Mercer regional manufacturing salary surveys, published hourly rates from contract manufacturers in target geographies.

Watch for: High-skill operations (precision welding, clean room assembly) that carry significant rate premiums. Automation levels that reduce labour but shift cost to depreciation. Overtime and shift premiums not reflected in base rates.

Manufacturing Overhead+

Overhead encompasses all costs not directly traceable to a unit: facility rent, utilities, equipment depreciation, maintenance, quality/inspection, indirect labour, and production management. Applied as a burden rate — typically expressed as a percentage of direct labour cost or machine hours.

Benchmark rates: Electronics contract manufacturers: 80–140% of DL. Precision machining: 150–250% of DL. High-volume plastics: 60–100% of DL. Rates vary by capital intensity and facility utilisation.

Watch for: Overhead allocation that buries inefficiencies. Suppliers running at low utilisation will spread higher overhead per unit — which is their problem to manage, not yours to subsidise indefinitely.

Tooling & NRE+

Non-Recurring Engineering (NRE) costs cover design, tooling, fixtures, test equipment, and first-article qualification — costs incurred once (or periodically) rather than per unit. These must be explicitly scoped and either paid upfront or amortised across the contracted volume. Ownership of tooling (buyer vs. supplier) has significant strategic implications.

Key questions: Who owns the tooling at end of life? What is the maintenance cost and responsibility? What happens to NRE if volume doesn't materialise? Is the NRE price competitive with independent quotes?

Strategic note: Buyer-owned tooling is transferable to an alternate supplier. Supplier-owned tooling creates lock-in. For strategic components, fight for tooling ownership — even if you pay more upfront.

Freight & Logistics+

Inbound freight to the supplier's facility, outbound freight to your dock, duty and customs duty (where applicable), packaging, warehousing, and insurance in transit. Often underestimated in should-cost models, particularly for heavy or oversized items, or for supply chains spanning multiple customs jurisdictions.

Key data sources: Freightos Baltic Index (container rates), IATA cargo rate indices, incoterms definitions for cost allocation, WTO tariff schedules for customs duty.

Watch for: Incoterms that shift freight risk in ways that obscure the true delivered cost. Suppliers bundling freight profit into a flat "DDP" price without transparency.

Supplier Margin & SG&A+

The final layer: selling, general & administrative overhead (typically 5–15% of revenue for manufacturers) plus net profit margin (typically 5–15%, higher for specialty or IP-intensive suppliers). Understanding industry benchmark margins — by segment and supplier type — tells you what is reasonable, and what represents excess return you should be competing away.

Benchmark sources: Published annual reports of public comparators, IBISWorld industry margin reports, Dun & Bradstreet industry financial benchmarks.

Key insight: A supplier with consistently high margins in a competitive category is either capturing genuine value (IP, quality, service premium) or has not faced sufficient competitive pressure. Understanding which is true determines your strategy.

Common Failure Modes

Should-cost modelling fails most often through poor inputs, misapplication, or isolation from the sourcing strategy it is meant to support.

01

Using the model as a weapon instead of a dialogue tool

Presenting a should-cost model as a demand — "our analysis says it should cost X, so that's what we'll pay" — invites suppliers to defend their position rather than engage with yours. It creates adversarialism and may cause capable suppliers to walk away from a commercially difficult negotiation.

Share the model as a starting point for discussion. Explicitly invite the supplier to challenge your assumptions. The conversation that follows is where value is created.
02

Building the model after the RFQ closes

A model built to challenge a specific supplier quote is reactive. It becomes a negotiating tool rather than a strategic input. The most powerful use of should-cost modelling is upstream: shaping specifications, setting target prices, and designing the sourcing event before any supplier sees the RFQ.

Integrate should-cost modelling into category planning. Build the model before the RFQ is issued — ideally while the specification is still being finalised.
03

Ignoring yield and scrap rates

A BOM priced at face value, without yield factors, will understate material cost by 5–20% depending on the process. Electronic PCB assembly, precision casting, and chemical processes all involve significant material loss. A model that ignores this will look analytically incompetent when challenged by a supplier.

Always apply process-specific yield factors to material quantities. Source industry benchmarks for first-pass yield by process type.
04

Using the wrong regional labour rate

Labour rates vary by a factor of 10–20× between low-cost and high-cost manufacturing geographies. A model built on a US rate applied to a Chinese supplier — or vice versa — is not useful. Ensure the rate reflects the actual (or expected) production location, fully loaded with employer contributions, benefits, and shift premiums.

Build region-specific models when geography is still a variable. Use country-level fully-loaded rates, not headline wages.
05

Treating the model as exact

A should-cost model is an estimate — a structured, data-driven estimate, but an estimate. It will have errors. Treating a ±15% accuracy model as a precise benchmark invites factual challenge. Suppliers will find the errors and use them to discredit the entire analysis.

Communicate confidence ranges honestly. A well-structured model with acknowledged uncertainty is more credible than a false-precision model that collapses under scrutiny.
06

Not updating the model as market conditions change

A model built 18 months ago using commodity prices from a different market cycle may be significantly wrong today. Copper, aluminium, rare earth metals, and semiconductor substrates can move 30–50% in a year. A stale model used in a live negotiation will be dismantled by a supplier with current data.

Update commodity pricing at every sourcing event. Set calendar reminders to refresh the model at key contract milestones or when a significant market movement occurs.
07

Excluding tooling and NRE from the analysis

Many should-cost models focus only on unit cost and ignore the lifecycle cost of tooling and NRE. This creates a misleading picture of total cost, particularly for custom components with high upfront investment. A unit cost optimisation that ignores NRE structure may actually increase total spend.

Always model NRE separately and as a per-unit amortised cost. Include sensitivity analysis for different volume scenarios — NRE per unit is highly sensitive to volume assumptions.
08

Procurement building the model in isolation

A should-cost model built without engineering input will have errors in process time, material specification, and technical feasibility that suppliers will exploit. Without finance input, the overhead and margin assumptions may not reflect industry reality. Isolation produces models that don't survive contact with the supplier.

Should-cost modelling is a cross-functional exercise. Define the team, schedule working sessions, and treat engineering and finance inputs as mandatory, not optional.

Supplier Selection & Evaluation

The structured process of identifying, assessing, and selecting the supplier most capable of meeting your requirements — commercially, technically, and strategically.

First Principles

Supplier selection is the decision that shapes your supply chain risk, quality baseline, and commercial leverage for years. It deserves the same rigour as any significant capital investment.

01

The supply base you choose today is tomorrow's constraint

Supplier selection decisions create structural dependencies that are expensive and slow to unwind. A wrong choice — a sole-source supplier that becomes financially distressed, a low-cost option that cannot meet quality — becomes a multi-year remediation programme. The time spent on rigorous selection is repaid many times over in avoided recovery cost.

02

Price is one criterion — not the criterion

A supplier selected on price alone is frequently the most expensive option when total cost is measured: quality failures, delivery disruption, rework, expediting, and relationship friction are not on the quote sheet. Weighted multi-criteria evaluation exists precisely to prevent price from collapsing a decision that involves many dimensions of value and risk.

03

Criteria must be defined before responses are received

Evaluation criteria set after reading supplier responses are inevitably shaped by those responses — consciously or not. Pre-defining and documenting criteria and weightings before any quotation is received is both ethically necessary and practically essential for defensible, auditable decisions.

04

Short-listing is strategic; scoring is operational

The long-list-to-short-list transition involves qualitative judgment about strategic fit, geographic footprint, financial health, and cultural alignment. This phase cannot be fully reduced to a scoring grid. Once at RFQ stage, consistent scored evaluation takes over. Both phases are necessary; conflating them produces poor decisions.

05

Due diligence is not optional for strategic supply

A questionnaire response tells you what a supplier wants you to believe. A site visit, financial health check, and reference call tell you what is actually true. For strategic or critical categories, documented due diligence is not risk-mitigation theatre — it is the difference between informed selection and informed hope.

06

The evaluation must be auditable

Supplier selection decisions are challenged — by unsuccessful suppliers, internal stakeholders, and auditors. Every evaluation must produce a documented trail: criteria, weightings, scores, evaluator names, and the rationale for the final decision. Verbal decisions and undocumented judgements are not defensible.

07

Onboarding begins at award, not after problems arise

The gap between award decision and operational readiness — approved supplier status, quality plans, capacity confirmation, systems integration, contractual execution — is where new supplier relationships most frequently fail. Formal onboarding management is the bridge between a good selection decision and a successful supply relationship.

08

The process should create competitive tension without wasting supplier resource

Running 12 suppliers through a full RFP process to select one is not competitive tension — it is waste. Long-list ruthlessly, short-list to 3–5, and invest your evaluation depth there. Suppliers that invest significant resource in an evaluation where they have no realistic chance will disengage from your next event.

A weighted multi-criteria model translates complex, multi-dimensional supplier differences into a structured, comparable assessment — reducing bias and creating a defensible audit trail.

Scoring Methodology

Anchored 1–5 Scale (Recommended)

Define what each score means for each criterion before evaluation begins. Example for On-Time Delivery: 5 = >98% OTD verified; 4 = 95–98%; 3 = 90–95%; 2 = 85–90%; 1 = <85% or unverifiable. Anchored scales eliminate inter-evaluator inconsistency and are far more defensible than unanchored numerical judgment.

Multiple Evaluators

Assign criteria to evaluators by domain expertise: engineering scores Technical Capability, finance scores Financial Health, operations scores Supply Chain. Average scores where multiple evaluators assess the same criterion. Document significant divergences — they often reveal genuine uncertainty about the supplier that the average obscures.

Weighting Governance

Criteria weights should be set by a cross-functional team and approved by the sourcing authority before the evaluation opens. Do not allow individual evaluators to set their own weights — this is the fastest way to introduce bias. For strategic sourcing decisions, document the weighting rationale.

RFI vs. RFQ vs. RFP

RFI (Request for Information): used for market scanning and pre-qualification — no pricing. RFQ (Request for Quotation): structured price and commercial response with defined specifications. RFP (Request for Proposal): used when the solution is not fully defined — supplier proposes an approach. Use the right instrument for the stage; mixing them creates confusion and poor responses.

Mandatory Qualifiers vs. Scored Criteria

Before scoring begins, establish pass/fail qualifiers — minimum thresholds a supplier must meet to remain in the evaluation. Suppliers failing a qualifier are disqualified regardless of their scores on other criteria.

Typical Qualifiers
  • Required certifications (ISO 9001, IATF 16949, etc.)
  • Minimum financial stability threshold
  • Regulatory and export compliance
  • Modern slavery / ESG policy in place
  • Capacity to meet minimum volume requirement
Why This Matters

Separating qualifiers from scored criteria prevents a supplier with a fatal flaw (e.g., no quality certification) from appearing attractive because they scored well on price. It also makes the evaluation process faster — disqualified suppliers are removed before detailed scoring.

Common Failure Modes

Supplier selection failures are most commonly process failures — criteria set too late, due diligence skipped, or onboarding neglected.

01

Writing the specification around the incumbent

Requirements written to match what your current supplier already does are not a specification — they are a disguised sole-source decision. This is both ethically problematic and operationally risky: it prevents the market from revealing better options and creates an incumbent dependency that is never stress-tested.

Requirements should describe the outcome needed, not the current supplier's approach. Have requirements reviewed by someone with no incumbent relationship before issue.
02

Setting evaluation criteria after receiving responses

Criteria designed after reading supplier responses will — consciously or unconsciously — favour responses the evaluator found impressive. This is bias, and it produces decisions that cannot be defended under audit or challenge from unsuccessful suppliers.

Criteria and weightings must be documented and approved before any supplier response is read. This is non-negotiable.
03

Selecting on price alone

The lowest-price supplier has the lowest direct material cost. They may also have the highest rework cost, the highest expediting frequency, the weakest financial position, and the shallowest engineering capability. Total cost of ownership — including all failure and friction costs — frequently reverses price-only rankings.

Use TCO-based commercial scoring, not unit price. Ensure the commercial domain weight (20–35%) does not dominate the evaluation at the expense of risk and capability criteria.
04

Skipping the site visit

A supplier's RFQ response is a marketing document. Their facility is reality. The gap between the two is often significant. Quality systems that look robust on paper can be absent in practice. Capacity claims that seem credible in a response can look implausible when you see the floor.

Site visits are mandatory for strategic and critical categories. For lower-value categories, desktop audits (virtual tours, third-party audit reports) are an acceptable alternative — but something must be done.
05

Ignoring financial health signals

A supplier that accepts your price, meets your quality requirements, and delivers on time — but fails financially 18 months into the contract — is a supply chain crisis. Financial health screening is not bureaucracy; it is supply continuity risk management.

Run a financial health check on every new strategic or critical supplier as part of due diligence. Re-run annually or at contract renewal. Define the financial health thresholds that trigger escalation or mitigation planning.
06

Too many suppliers in the final evaluation

Running 8 suppliers through a full RFP/evaluation/BAFO process wastes everyone's time and signals that you are not serious about the decision. It also incentivises suppliers to submit safe, uncommitted bids rather than their best and most innovative proposal.

Long-list aggressively; short-list ruthlessly. 3–5 suppliers is the right number for a full evaluation. Use the RFI stage to get there efficiently.
07

Weak onboarding after award

The period between contract award and first production delivery is where many new supplier relationships fail. Without a structured onboarding plan, quality issues, system integration problems, and communication gaps accumulate before the relationship has built the trust needed to resolve them quickly.

Treat supplier onboarding as a project with a named owner, a plan, milestones, and a 90-day review. The first 90 days set the tone for the entire relationship.
08

No documented rationale for the award decision

A supplier selection decision that cannot be explained clearly — including why the selected supplier was preferred over specific alternatives — is not defensible. Internal stakeholders will challenge it. Unsuccessful suppliers will question it. Auditors will flag it.

Document the final evaluation matrix, the scoring rationale for key criteria, and the decision meeting minutes. The award letter to the successful supplier should reference the selection process. Keep the documentation for at least the life of the contract.

Supplier Relationship Management

The deliberate programme of differentiated engagement with strategically important suppliers — designed to create mutual value that transactional management cannot reach.

First Principles

SRM is not general supplier management — it is a targeted investment in a small number of relationships where that investment generates returns unavailable through transactional engagement.

01

SRM is not for all suppliers

The defining characteristic of SRM is selectivity. Applied to all suppliers, it becomes general management — the differentiation disappears, the investment is diluted, and the returns evaporate. The value of SRM comes precisely from concentrating disproportionate relationship investment in the 5–15 suppliers where that investment generates asymmetric returns.

02

SRM is mutually beneficial — not unilaterally demanding

Organisations that treat SRM as a programme for extracting more from suppliers while giving nothing in return will fail. Strategic suppliers are sophisticated enough to recognise one-sided relationships and will respond in kind: less transparency, less innovation sharing, less discretionary effort in a crisis. Genuine SRM creates value for both parties.

03

It must be institutionalised, not personalised

An SRM programme that exists in the relationship between two individuals ends when either individual leaves. The programme must be embedded in governance structures, documented in processes, and owned by the organisation — not the person. Supplier engagement should survive personnel changes on both sides.

04

SRM ≠ contract management

Contract management is the discipline of executing and enforcing a signed agreement. SRM operates above and beyond the contract: it is about building the trust, transparency, and joint capability that allow both parties to do more together than the contract requires. Confusing the two produces compliance-focused relationships that cannot generate innovation or resilience.

05

Executive sponsorship transforms the relationship

The presence of executive sponsors on both sides — who meet regularly, who take a genuine interest in the relationship's health, and who can unblock problems that operational teams cannot — is the single biggest differentiator between SRM programmes that deliver innovation and those that deliver only performance reviews.

06

Scorecards are the floor, not the ceiling

Performance scorecards measure compliance with existing standards. The strategic value in SRM comes from what happens above the scorecard: joint process improvement, early access to new technology, co-investment in capability, and preferential allocation during scarcity. These outcomes require relationship depth that scorecard management alone cannot produce.

07

You need to know the supplier's strategy, not just their performance

A strategic supplier's investment priorities, technology roadmap, customer portfolio strategy, and geographic expansion plans all affect your supply security. SRM creates the forum where this information is shared voluntarily — because the relationship has earned it. Without it, you discover strategic shifts only when they have already affected your supply.

08

Exit from the strategic tier must be possible

Strategic classification is not permanent. A supplier whose strategic importance has diminished, whose performance has persistently underperformed, or who has been replaced by a superior alternative should be reclassified. An SRM programme without exit criteria becomes a list of sacred cows — relationships maintained out of inertia rather than strategic value.

Segmentation Model

SRM begins with honest segmentation — determining which suppliers warrant which level of engagement. Most organisations over-classify; the discipline is in being selective.

The Four-Tier Model

Tier 1 — Strategic

5–15 suppliers. High spend, critical supply, innovation potential, or deep interdependency. Sole or near-sole source. Replacement would take 12+ months.

Full SRM programme. Executive sponsors. Quarterly strategic reviews. JVP / roadmap sharing.
Tier 2 — Preferred

15–40 suppliers. Significant spend, qualified alternatives exist but switching has meaningful cost and disruption. Proactively managed.

Active category management. Quarterly performance reviews. Preferred status with volume allocation. Development discussions.
Tier 3 — Approved

Lower spend, easily substituted, or low criticality. Qualified and approved but not actively managed beyond transactional performance.

Annual review. Contract compliance monitoring. Reactive issue management. No dedicated relationship investment.
Tier 4 — Transactional

Commodity, low-value, or spot-purchase suppliers. No strategic dependency. Managed purely on price and availability.

Catalogue / P-card / automated procurement. Minimal touch. Periodic rationalisation to reduce supplier base complexity.

Common Failure Modes

SRM programmes fail most often through over-ambition, under-investment, or confusion between performance management and genuine relationship development.

01

Too many strategic suppliers

The most universal SRM failure. When the strategic tier contains 50 suppliers, no relationship receives the investment that makes SRM valuable. Everyone is in the programme; no one benefits from it. The label "strategic" becomes meaningless — applied by political convenience rather than analytical rigour.

Cap the strategic tier at a number your team can genuinely invest in: typically 5–15 for most procurement teams. Use the segmentation criteria deliberately and be willing to have difficult conversations about reclassification.
02

SRM as a one-way value extraction programme

Programmes framed internally as "getting more from strategic suppliers" — lower prices, shorter lead times, better terms — without any articulation of what the buyer is offering in return will be seen through by suppliers immediately. Strategic suppliers will comply minimally and invest their discretionary effort elsewhere.

Define explicitly what you will give strategic suppliers: volume commitment, forecast visibility, early design access, joint development funding, payment improvements. Make the programme genuinely bilateral.
03

Scorecard theatre — metrics with no consequences

Scorecards produced quarterly that are read, filed, and never acted upon teach suppliers that performance measurement is an administrative exercise. Persistent underperformance without response signals that the scorecard is not real. Suppliers manage what they believe buyers actually care about.

Define clear response protocols for different performance levels: a score below threshold triggers a formal improvement plan with a defined timeline; persistent failure below threshold triggers supply base review. Make consequences real and consistently applied.
04

Relationship that lives in one person

The category manager who has managed the relationship for five years leaves. The institutional knowledge, the mutual trust, the informal information sharing — all go with them. The supplier's counterpart also changes. Two years later, the relationship exists on paper but has no substance.

Document relationship history, key contacts, past agreements, and relationship health assessments in a system of record. Require formal handovers for all strategic supplier relationships. Involve more than one person from each organisation in regular engagement.
05

No executive engagement — operational SRM only

A programme that runs exclusively at the operational level cannot achieve the strategic alignment that justifies the investment. Executive sponsors signal that the relationship is genuinely important to the organisation. Without them, the supplier's senior leadership will invest their attention in customers who have taken that step.

Assign an executive sponsor for every Tier 1 supplier. Define their role, cadence, and accountabilities. Brief them thoroughly before each supplier interaction so they can engage substantively.
06

Never asking the supplier how the relationship looks from their side

Organisations that measure supplier performance but never assess their own performance as a buyer are operating on half the data. Forecast inaccuracy, specification changes, late payments, and poor communication are buyer failures that damage supply performance — and that suppliers rarely raise voluntarily.

Formally survey or discuss supplier satisfaction with your organisation annually. Act on what you learn. Suppliers notice when buyers follow through on self-improvement — it builds trust.
07

Confusing SRM with contract management

Contract management is about compliance and obligation. SRM is about value creation and relationship health. Running quarterly contract compliance reviews and calling it SRM misses the point entirely. Performance management is the floor; strategic collaboration is the ceiling. Most programmes never get off the floor.

Structure your engagement model to include both: operational/contract compliance tracking AND strategic value creation discussions. They require different conversations, different participants, and different mindsets. Don't conflate them in the same meeting.
08

No mechanism for strategic reclassification

A supplier that was strategic three years ago may no longer be — their category has been commoditised, alternatives have been qualified, or their performance has deteriorated below recovery. Keeping them in the strategic tier out of inertia wastes investment and sends the wrong signal to suppliers who have genuinely earned that status.

Review strategic classification annually. Define explicit exit criteria — performance thresholds, strategic relevance changes, supply base developments. Reclassification down is a management action, not a relationship failure.

Supply Market Analysis

The structured investigation of external market forces — supplier landscape, competitive dynamics, cost drivers, and macro trends — that shape what is strategically possible in a category.

First Principles

Category strategy without supply market intelligence is opinion. Supply market analysis converts the external environment from a blind spot into a structural input to every sourcing and negotiation decision.

01

You cannot negotiate what you do not understand

A procurement professional who doesn't understand supplier cost structures, market concentration, capacity utilisation, and competitive dynamics is negotiating from a position of structural ignorance. Supply market analysis is the intelligence foundation that converts negotiation from guesswork into informed strategy. It is not optional for any category of significance.

02

Market analysis precedes category strategy — not the other way around

A category strategy that is not grounded in market reality will fail at execution. The market determines what is strategically possible: whether genuine competition can be created, whether dual-sourcing is feasible, whether there is a window to renegotiate, whether the market is tightening or loosening. Strategy must be calibrated to reality before it is committed to paper.

03

Markets change — analysis must be periodic

A supply market analysis conducted three years ago describes a market that no longer exists in the same form. Commodity prices, supplier consolidation, geopolitical shifts, regulatory changes, and technology disruptions can fundamentally alter a market in 12–18 months. Analysis must be refreshed before every major sourcing event and at a minimum annually for strategic categories.

04

Supplier economics drive supplier behaviour

Understanding what it costs a supplier to serve you — and what margin they are making — explains their behaviour in ways that no amount of relationship management can reveal. A supplier operating at thin margins under capacity pressure will behave differently from one with strong margins and idle capacity. Supplier economics are your most actionable intelligence.

05

Concentration is the most important structural risk

A market served by two or three suppliers is structurally different from one served by twenty. High concentration gives supplier power, limits competitive tension, and creates fragility — a single supplier failure can have no market-based solution. Measuring and tracking concentration (via the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index or simpler supplier count) is a fundamental risk management activity.

06

Technology disruption creates both risk and opportunity

Where a category sits on the technology lifecycle — emerging, mature, or declining — fundamentally affects the sourcing strategy. A component moving toward obsolescence requires a redesign strategy. A category where new manufacturing technology is emerging may offer step-change cost reduction if you can access the right suppliers early.

07

External forces affect supplier costs independently of their choices

Commodity price inflation, regulatory compliance costs, currency movements, energy prices, and labour market tightness all affect supplier costs regardless of their operational efficiency. Understanding which cost increases are externally driven (legitimate) versus internally driven (efficiency failures or margin expansion) is essential for fair and effective price management.

08

Market intelligence is a strategic asset — treat it as one

Most procurement teams conduct supply market analysis reactively — when a sourcing event is imminent. Leading functions build ongoing market intelligence capabilities: tracking commodity indices, monitoring supplier news and financial results, maintaining relationships with industry analysts, and attending relevant trade events. This continuous intelligence flow provides advantage in every commercial interaction.

Analytical Frameworks

Five lenses for understanding a supply market — from competitive structure to macro forces to the supplier landscape itself. Used in combination, they reveal the full picture.

Porter's Five Forces — Adapted for Procurement

Supplier Power

The core force. Driven by: number of qualified suppliers, switching cost, degree of differentiation, importance of the buyer to the supplier, and concentration of the supply market. High supplier power = your leverage is limited; strategy must focus on reducing dependency or increasing competition.

Buyer Power

Your leverage in the market. Influenced by: your share of supplier revenue, ability to backward integrate, number of alternatives available, volume and consistency of demand. Assess both your absolute power and your power relative to other customers of the same supplier.

Threat of Substitution

Can the function or performance requirement be met by a different technology, material, or approach? High substitution threat weakens supplier power and opens design-to-cost opportunities. Low substitution (e.g., a safety-critical proprietary component) reinforces supplier power.

Threat of New Entrants

Are new suppliers likely to enter the market? Driven by: barriers to entry (capital, certifications, IP, scale), profitability attractiveness, and market growth rate. High entry threat disciplines existing supplier pricing; low entry threat (e.g., defence-qualified electronics) entrenches existing power dynamics.

Competitive Rivalry

How aggressively do existing suppliers compete for business? In concentrated markets with few players, rivalry may be limited (cartel-like behaviour, if not literally a cartel). In fragmented markets, aggressive competition can be exploited to drive price discipline. Capacity utilisation is the key indicator — low utilisation intensifies rivalry.

Supply/Demand Dynamics & Supplier Landscape

Capacity & Utilisation

Capacity utilisation is the single most predictive indicator of market tightness and pricing direction. Industry utilisation above ~85% signals tightening supply, lengthening lead times, and increasing supplier pricing power. Below ~75%, suppliers have idle capacity and incentive to compete aggressively for volume. Track reported utilisation for key supply markets.

Supplier Landscape Map

For each strategic category, map the qualified supplier universe: number of suppliers globally, regionally, and currently qualified to your standards. Calculate the Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) as a concentration measure. An HHI above 2,500 indicates a highly concentrated market with significant supplier power implications.

Price Trend Analysis

Track price history for the category against relevant indices. Separate commodity-driven movements (external, legitimately passed through) from above-index movements (potential margin expansion). A supplier whose price has risen 15% against a commodity index that has risen 8% owes an explanation for the differential — your should-cost model is the tool to investigate it.

Technology Roadmap Position

Assess where the category sits on the technology lifecycle and which suppliers are best positioned for the next generation of technology. A current-generation sole source that is not investing in next-generation capability creates a transition risk. Early engagement with emerging technology suppliers — even before they are qualified — is a supply market strategy, not premature switching.

Common Failure Modes

Supply market analysis fails most often when it produces interesting intelligence that is never connected to a decision — or when it is conducted too late to influence the sourcing strategy.

01

One-and-done analysis — never refreshed

A supply market analysis conducted at category strategy launch and never revisited becomes a historical document masquerading as current intelligence. Markets move — sometimes dramatically and quickly. A procurement team operating from 2-year-old market analysis in a category affected by geopolitical disruption, commodity volatility, or technological change is flying blind.

Set a calendar for annual analysis refresh for all strategic categories. Trigger an immediate refresh whenever a significant market event occurs: a major supplier failure, a commodity price move >20%, a significant regulatory change, or a geopolitical shock in a key supply geography.
02

Sourcing only from already-known suppliers

If your supply market analysis identifies only the suppliers you already know, it is not an analysis — it is a rationalisation of the status quo. The entire point of market mapping is to reveal suppliers you have not yet evaluated, including those who might offer step-change cost, quality, or capability improvements.

Require the analysis to identify at least three suppliers not currently in your approved supply base. Follow up with RFI outreach to the most credible candidates — even if you don't intend to switch, the competitive intelligence is valuable.
03

Accepting the supplier's price increase narrative uncritically

Every supplier price increase comes with a story: raw material inflation, labour cost, energy, regulatory compliance. Some of these stories are accurate. Others are partial truths, or deliberately constructed to justify above-market increases. Without independent market data, you cannot distinguish legitimate cost pass-through from margin expansion.

Build a standard price change analysis template that compares the supplier's claimed drivers against relevant indices. Any increase above the relevant benchmark must be specifically justified with supplier cost data, not just asserted.
04

Ignoring sub-tier and geographic concentration

Your Tier 1 supplier may appear diversified — but if three of your four Tier 1 suppliers source the same critical raw material from the same two sub-tier suppliers in the same country, your supply chain has a single point of failure that doesn't show on any Tier 1 analysis. Geographic concentration at Tier 2+ is a structural risk most organisations discover only when it becomes a crisis.

For critical categories, map supply chain dependencies to at least Tier 2. Identify geographic and supplier concentration at each tier. Include this in your risk register and design mitigation strategies accordingly.
05

Analysis paralysis — never translating insight to strategy

Some procurement teams produce thorough, well-researched market analyses that are read with interest, discussed in category meetings, and then filed. The analysis never produces a changed sourcing strategy, a renegotiation, a new supplier qualification, or a risk mitigation action. Intelligence that doesn't change behaviour has no commercial value.

Require every supply market analysis to conclude with a minimum of three explicit strategy implications or action items, each with an owner and deadline. Gate category strategy approval on evidence that market analysis was used to shape it.
06

Conflating market price with supplier cost

The market price of a commodity (copper at $9,500/tonne) is not the same as the cost of copper in a supplier's product. Processing yield, alloy specification, form factor, and minimum order quantities all affect the actual material cost. And material cost is only one element of total product cost. Treating commodity price movements as directly proportional to product price changes oversimplifies a complex relationship.

Use should-cost modelling to quantify the actual impact of a commodity price movement on a specific product's cost. Track the relevant commodity as a share of total product cost — a 20% copper price movement in a product where copper is 15% of cost is a 3% product cost impact, not 20%.
07

No view of supplier financial health at market level

Individual supplier financial health checks are important. But supply market analysis should also assess the financial health of the market as a whole: are suppliers in this category broadly profitable? Is the industry consolidating because margins are too thin to sustain fragmentation? Are there suppliers showing stress signals that might precipitate failure or distress M&A?

Include industry financial health in the market analysis: review public financial data for listed suppliers, track M&A activity in the sector, and monitor industry-level margin trends. Use this to anticipate supply base changes before they become disruptions.
08

Conducting analysis too late to influence sourcing strategy

Supply market analysis initiated the week before a sourcing event opens cannot meaningfully influence the strategy: there is no time to qualify new suppliers, adjust specifications, or reconsider the sourcing model. The analysis becomes a post-hoc justification for a decision already made by calendar pressure.

Integrate supply market analysis into the category planning calendar — it must be completed at least 3–6 months before a major sourcing event. For strategic categories with long qualification cycles, start earlier. The analysis is an input to strategy, not a deliverable produced alongside it.

Procurement
Name the stages of the P2P cycle in order.
Need identification → Requisition → Sourcing/supplier selection → Purchase order → Goods receipt → Invoice matching (3-way match: PO, receipt, invoice) → Payment. The 3-way match is the core financial control in the cycle.
Procurement
When do you use an RFI vs. RFQ vs. RFP — and what's the core difference?
RFI: market scanning and pre-qualification — gathering information, no pricing. RFQ: price and commercial terms against a fully defined specification. RFP: the solution is not fully defined — suppliers propose their approach. The core difference is specification maturity: the more defined your requirement, the closer to RFQ you move.
Procurement
What's the difference between cost reduction and cost avoidance — and why does the distinction matter?
Cost reduction lowers actual spend against a historical baseline (visible in the P&L). Cost avoidance prevents an increase that would otherwise have occurred (invisible in the P&L). The distinction matters because finance only credits what it can see — conflating the two damages procurement's credibility and inflates reported savings.
Category Management
Draw the Kraljic Matrix from memory: axes, four quadrants, and the strategy for each.
Axes: supply risk (x) × profit impact (y). Strategic (high/high): partner deeply, secure long-term. Leverage (low risk/high impact): create competition, drive price. Bottleneck (high risk/low impact): secure continuity, reduce dependency, hold buffer. Routine (low/low): automate, minimise transaction cost, rationalise.

Name the seven TCO cost buckets.

TCO Model
Why does TCO analysis frequently reverse a price-based supplier ranking?
Because the quote sheet captures only acquisition cost — typically 60–80% of total cost. A lower-priced supplier with higher defect rates, longer lead times (more inventory), less reliable delivery (expediting costs), and weaker financial stability (switching risk) accumulates costs across the other six buckets that exceed the price advantage. The cheapest quote is frequently the most expensive supplier.
Negotiation
Define BATNA and ZOPA, and explain how they relate.
BATNA: your Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement — what you'll actually do if this deal fails. It defines your walkaway point and is the true source of negotiating power. ZOPA: the Zone Of Possible Agreement — the overlap between your walkaway and theirs. If BATNA improves, your walkaway hardens, and the ZOPA shifts in your favour. No BATNA = negotiating on hope.
Negotiation
State the three concession rules.
1. Never free — every concession must be traded for something of value in return. 2. Always shrinking — each successive concession should be smaller than the last, signalling you're approaching your limit. 3. Always slower — increasing time between concessions reinforces the same signal. Violating these teaches the counterparty that pushing harder produces more.
Negotiation
What are the three positions in the position stack, and when must they be set?
Opening (ambitious but justifiable — anchors the discussion) · Target (your realistic goal — where you expect to land) · Walkaway (the point you leave, defined by your BATNA). All three must be set before the negotiation begins — a walkaway invented mid-meeting under pressure isn't a walkaway, it's a rationalisation.

The erosion of negotiated value after signature through passive management. Industry estimates put typical leakage at a significant share of negotiated value when contracts are unmanaged.

What are the three dimensions of the spend cube, and the seven process steps?
Cube: Category × Supplier × Business unit — every analysis is a slice through these three. Process: Extract → Consolidate → Cleanse → Classify → Enrich → Analyse → Action. The most commonly skipped steps are cleanse/classify (garbage in, garbage out) and action (analysis that changes nothing).
Spend Analysis
Name four analytical lenses you'd apply to a cleansed spend dataset.
Supplier concentration (dependency risk, consolidation opportunity) · Tail spend (long tail of small suppliers — rationalisation) · Maverick spend (off-contract buying — compliance leakage) · Price variance (same item, different prices across units) · Payment terms distribution (working capital opportunity) · Contract coverage (spend under management %).
Should-Cost
Name the six cost layers of a should-cost model.
Direct materials (BOM × market pricing × yield factor) · Direct labour (process time × regional loaded rate) · Manufacturing overhead (burden rate on labour/machine hours) · Tooling & NRE (amortised over volume) · Freight & logistics · SG&A + fair profit margin. Built from market data, independent of any supplier quote.
Should-Cost
A supplier quotes 40% above your should-cost model. What are the two possible explanations, and what do you do?
Either (a) the supplier's price contains excess margin or inefficiency you shouldn't fund, or (b) your model has gaps — constraints, capabilities, or costs it doesn't capture. Both are valuable findings. Action: share the model, invite the supplier to identify where your assumptions are wrong, and let the resulting conversation either improve your model or drive price down. The gap is a data point demanding explanation, not a verdict.
Supplier Selection
Why must evaluation criteria be defined before responses are received — and what's the difference between a qualifier and a scored criterion?
Criteria set after reading responses are shaped by those responses — bias that makes the decision indefensible under audit or supplier challenge. Qualifiers are pass/fail minimums (certifications, financial stability, compliance) — failing one disqualifies regardless of other scores. Scored criteria differentiate among qualified suppliers. Separating them prevents a fatally flawed supplier from looking attractive on price.
Supplier Selection
Recite the eight-stage selection funnel.
Define (requirements + criteria, pre-approved) → Long list (market scan) → Pre-qualify (RFI + qualifiers → short list) → Solicit (RFQ/RFP) → Evaluate (scored against pre-set criteria) → Clarify/BAFO → Due diligence (site visit, financial check, references) → Award & onboard (with named owner and 90-day review).
SRM
Why cap the strategic supplier tier at 5–15, and what happens when you don't?
SRM's value comes from concentrating disproportionate relationship investment where it generates asymmetric returns — executive sponsorship, QBRs, joint value creation all consume real capacity. Spread across 50 "strategic" suppliers, the investment per relationship falls below the threshold where it produces returns. Everyone is in the programme; no one benefits from it. The label becomes political rather than analytical.
SRM
Describe the three-level governance cadence for a strategic supplier.
Operational — monthly, ops leads both sides: KPIs, open issues, order pipeline. Strategic (QBR) — quarterly, category manager + senior account manager: scorecard, improvement initiatives, market outlook, relationship health. Executive — annual or bi-annual, CPO + supplier leadership: strategy alignment, innovation pipeline, joint value roadmap. Each level has distinct participants, agenda, and outputs — don't conflate them.
Market Analysis
Name Porter's Five Forces as adapted for procurement, and identify which is the core force.
Supplier power (the core force — number of alternatives, switching cost, differentiation, concentration) · Buyer power (your share of their revenue, alternatives available) · Threat of substitution (different technology/material meeting the same need) · Threat of new entrants (barriers to entry, market attractiveness) · Competitive rivalry (how aggressively suppliers compete — capacity utilisation is the key indicator).
Market Analysis
What is the single most predictive indicator of market tightness, and what are the thresholds?
Capacity utilisation. Above ~85%: tightening market — lengthening lead times, rising supplier pricing power; lock in supply and expect price pressure. Below ~75%: suppliers have idle capacity and incentive to compete aggressively — a buyer's window for renegotiation and sourcing events. Timing sourcing activity against the utilisation cycle is one of the most underused levers in procurement.
Market Analysis
Your supplier claims a 15% price increase is "raw material driven." How do you test the claim?
Compare against the relevant commodity index weighted by that material's share of product cost. If copper rose 20% but copper is 15% of product cost, the justified impact is ~3% — not 15%. Any increase above the index-weighted impact requires specific supplier cost data, not assertion. This is where your should-cost model and market analysis converge into a single negotiation instrument.

Inventory & Planning Interface

Where procurement meets the planning engine — the mechanics of how demand signals, inventory policy, and supplier lead times interact to determine what gets bought, when, and in what quantity.

First Principles

Inventory is money in physical form, sitting on a shelf. Every inventory decision is a trade-off between service, cost, and risk — and procurement sits at the point where those trade-offs are executed with suppliers.

01

Inventory exists to decouple supply from demand

Inventory is a buffer against mismatched timing: suppliers cannot deliver instantly, demand is not perfectly predictable, and production runs in batches. Every unit of stock exists to absorb one of these mismatches. Understanding which mismatch a given inventory pool serves is the prerequisite to deciding how much of it you need.

02

Service level is a choice, not an outcome

The stock-out probability you accept is a deliberate commercial decision — a 95% service level requires far less safety stock than 99%, and the cost difference is non-linear. The service level should be set by item criticality and the cost of a stock-out, not applied uniformly across the portfolio.

03

Lead time is the master variable

Almost every inventory quantity scales with lead time: reorder points, safety stock, exposure to obsolescence, and forecast error all grow as lead time extends. A supplier lead time reduction from 12 weeks to 6 is not a convenience — it halves your exposure window and can release significant working capital. Lead time is a negotiable commercial term; treat it as one.

04

Variability costs more than volume

Stable demand of 1,000 units/month is cheap to serve. Demand that averages 1,000 but swings between 200 and 2,500 is expensive — it drives safety stock, expediting, and capacity buffers throughout the chain. Reducing variability (through forecast sharing, order smoothing, and contract structure) is often worth more than reducing average volume cost.

05

MOQs and price breaks are inventory decisions in disguise

A supplier's minimum order quantity or volume price break transfers inventory holding from their balance sheet to yours. The unit price saving must be weighed against carrying cost, obsolescence risk, and cash committed. A 5% price break that forces 9 months of stock is frequently a net loss — do the arithmetic every time.

06

Not all SKUs deserve equal attention

ABC analysis (by value) crossed with XYZ analysis (by demand variability) segments the portfolio into management classes. AX items (high value, stable) suit tight automated replenishment; AZ items (high value, erratic) need human planning judgment; CX items (low value, stable) should consume near-zero planning effort. Uniform policies waste effort where it isn't needed and underinvest where it is.

07

Obsolescence is the silent inventory killer

In electronics especially, component lifecycles are shorter than product lifecycles. Excess stock doesn't just carry cost — it can become worthless overnight with an engineering change or end-of-life notice. Last-time-buy decisions are among the highest-stakes inventory calls procurement makes: buy too little and face a redesign; buy too much and write it off.

08

The forecast is always wrong — the question is by how much

Plan around forecast error, not forecast accuracy. Measure it (MAPE, bias), track it by item class, and size buffers from measured error rather than assumed precision. A planning system that pretends the forecast is right will systematically under-protect volatile items and over-protect stable ones.

Core Mechanics

The quantitative machinery of replenishment. These models are simplifications — their value is in making the trade-offs explicit, not in producing decimal-point precision.

Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)+

EOQ = √(2DS ÷ H) — where D is annual demand, S is the fixed cost per order, and H is the annual holding cost per unit. It finds the order size that minimises the sum of ordering cost and carrying cost.

Use it for: a starting point on order sizing, and as the counterweight when a supplier pushes MOQs or price breaks above your economic quantity.

Its limits: assumes stable demand, fixed costs, and no quantity discounts. Real decisions adjust EOQ for MOQs, packaging multiples, shelf life, and price break economics — but the EOQ tells you what the adjustment is costing you.

Safety Stock & Service Level+

Basic form: SS = Z × σd × √LT — where Z is the service factor (1.65 for 95%, 2.33 for 99%), σd is the standard deviation of demand per period, and LT is lead time in periods. Extended forms also incorporate lead time variability, which often matters more than demand variability.

Key insight: safety stock scales with the square root of lead time but linearly with the Z factor — pushing service from 95% to 99% costs ~40% more buffer; pushing to 99.9% roughly doubles it. Choose service levels item by item.

Procurement lever: reducing supplier lead time variability (not just average lead time) directly cuts required safety stock. Make delivery consistency a scored KPI, not just OTD.

Reorder Point (ROP)+

ROP = (average demand per period × lead time in periods) + safety stock. When stock position (on hand + on order − allocated) falls to the ROP, a replenishment order triggers.

Watch: the calculation uses stock position, not stock on hand — forgetting open POs causes double-ordering. And every lead time change must flow through to the ROP immediately; stale lead times are the most common cause of systematic stock-outs after a supply transition.

ABC / XYZ Segmentation+

ABC ranks items by annual spend value (A ≈ top 80% of value, B next 15%, C last 5%). XYZ ranks by demand variability (X stable, Y seasonal/trending, Z erratic). The 9-cell matrix assigns planning policy: automated min/max for CX, statistical replenishment for AX/BX, human review and supplier collaboration for AZ/BZ.

Procurement application: AX items justify long-term agreements with scheduled releases; AZ items justify flexibility clauses and vendor-managed buffers; CX items belong on catalogues or consignment with zero planning touch.

MOQ & Price Break Economics+

To evaluate a price break: compare the unit price saving on annual volume against the incremental carrying cost of the larger order quantity — incremental average inventory × holding rate (typically 15–25%/yr for electronics once obsolescence and cost of capital are included). Include obsolescence probability explicitly for lifecycle-sensitive parts.

Rule of thumb: if the price break forces more than ~3–4 months of additional stock for a saving under 5%, it usually fails the arithmetic. Counter-offer: the break price at a committed annual volume with scheduled releases — the supplier gets the volume certainty; you avoid the inventory.

VMI, Consignment & Buffer Agreements+

Vendor-Managed Inventory: the supplier plans and replenishes against agreed min/max levels using your consumption data. Consignment: stock sits at your site but remains supplier-owned until consumed — you carry no working capital. Buffer agreements: the supplier holds finished or semi-finished stock at agreed levels, funded by volume commitment or a holding fee.

When each fits: VMI for stable, repetitive C/B items where planning effort exceeds value; consignment for high-value items with unpredictable pull; buffer agreements for long-lead strategic components where continuity risk justifies the commitment.

The honest trade: all three shift cost or risk to the supplier — expect it priced in somewhere. The win is when the supplier can carry the buffer more cheaply than you can (better demand pooling, earlier position in the value chain), creating genuine joint saving rather than cost transfer.

Last-Time-Buy (LTB) Decisions+

When a component is end-of-life'd, the LTB quantity must cover remaining product demand plus service/warranty obligations across the support horizon — often 5–10 years for industrial electronics. Build the estimate from: remaining production forecast, field failure rates for service demand, yield loss, and a defined confidence buffer.

Before buying: exhaust the alternatives — pin-compatible substitutes, broker market assessment, die-bank options, or redesign economics. An LTB is a multi-year bet with zero salvage value if wrong; it deserves cross-functional sign-off (engineering + product + finance), not a solo procurement call.

Common Failure Modes

Planning-interface failures are quiet — they show up as chronic expediting, creeping stock, and write-offs, rarely as a single visible event.

01

Chasing unit price into an inventory trap

Accepting MOQs or price breaks far above economic quantity converts a visible price saving into invisible carrying cost and obsolescence exposure. Procurement gets credited for the saving; the write-off two years later lands on someone else's line.

Run the price-break arithmetic (saving vs. incremental carrying + obsolescence cost) on every MOQ above ~3 months of demand. Report deals on a total-cost basis, not unit price.
02

Uniform service levels across the portfolio

Applying 98% service to every SKU over-protects cheap stable items and under-protects critical volatile ones. The result is simultaneously too much inventory and too many of the stock-outs that actually hurt.

Set service levels by ABC/XYZ class and stock-out consequence. Review annually — item criticality changes as products and suppliers change.
03

Stale lead times in the planning system

Lead times entered at qualification and never updated silently corrupt every reorder point and safety stock downstream. After a supply market shift, the system keeps ordering as if it were still last year.

Reconcile system lead times against actual receipt performance quarterly. Make lead time updates a mandatory step in every sourcing change and supplier review.
04

Forecast sharing without firmness bands

Sending suppliers a raw forecast with no definition of what is firm creates one of two failures: the supplier treats it all as speculative (no capacity protection), or claims it was all commitment (liability dispute when demand drops).

Contractually define the bands — firm, material-authorised, forecast-only — with explicit liability at each horizon. Review band performance at QBRs.
05

Treating expediting as free

Chronic expediting — premium freight, broker buys, schedule disruption — is a recurring tax paid for planning failure. Because it's spread across many small events, it rarely appears as a line item anyone owns.

Track expedite spend and premium freight as a named metric with a root-cause code (forecast error, supplier failure, parameter error). Chronic causes get fixed; one-off events get absorbed.
06

LTB decisions made by one function

A last-time-buy sized by procurement alone (or engineering alone) systematically misses either the demand picture, the substitution options, or the financial exposure. These are multi-year, zero-salvage bets.

Formalise LTB as a cross-functional decision — engineering (alternatives), product (demand horizon), finance (exposure), procurement (market options) — with documented assumptions and sign-off.
07

Consignment/VMI as cost transfer theatre

Pushing inventory onto supplier books without changing total chain cost just relocates the expense — and it returns in price at the next negotiation, with a margin on top.

Pursue VMI/consignment where the supplier genuinely carries stock cheaper (demand pooling, upstream position). Model the total chain cost before and after; if it hasn't fallen, you've moved cost, not removed it.
08

Ignoring demand variability you are causing

Batch ordering, end-of-quarter pushes, and lumpy release patterns amplify variability up the chain (the bullwhip effect). Suppliers price that chaos back to you as lead time, buffer stock, and margin.

Smooth release patterns where demand allows. Ask key suppliers where your ordering behaviour hurts them — the answer is often a free lead-time or cost improvement.

Supply Chain Resilience

The deliberate design of a supply chain that can absorb, adapt to, and recover from disruption — built before the event, because during the event is too late.

First Principles

Resilience is distinct from risk management: risk management asks "what could go wrong and how likely is it?" Resilience asks "when something goes wrong — predicted or not — how fast do we recover?"

01

Efficiency and resilience trade off — choose consciously

Decades of lean optimisation stripped buffers, consolidated suppliers, and extended supply lines — all rational for cost, all fragilising. Neither extreme is right. The discipline is making the trade-off explicitly, item by item, rather than discovering after a disruption that efficiency was chosen by default everywhere.

02

You cannot protect what you cannot see

Most organisations know their Tier 1 suppliers and almost nothing beyond. Yet disruptions overwhelmingly originate at Tier 2+ — the fab, the substrate maker, the specialty chemical plant that three of your "diversified" Tier 1s all depend on. Multi-tier mapping of critical paths is the entry ticket to resilience; everything else builds on it.

03

Time-to-recover and time-to-survive frame every decision

For each critical node ask: if it fails, how long until supply resumes (TTR), and how long can we operate without it (TTS)? Where TTR exceeds TTS, you have quantified exposure — and a case for buffers, alternates, or redesign. This simple comparison converts vague anxiety into a ranked investment list.

04

Dual sourcing is a capability, not a line in a register

A second source that has never received volume, holds no tooling, and hasn't been qualified on the current revision is a name, not an alternative. Genuine dual sourcing means both suppliers are qualified, tooled, and receiving enough share to stay current — which costs money in duplicated NRE and split leverage. Pay it only where the exposure justifies it.

05

Concentration hides inside diversification

Four suppliers across three countries can still share one upstream chokepoint — a single wafer fab, one port, one rare-earth refiner. Resilience analysis must trace to the level where paths genuinely diverge, not stop at the count of Tier 1 logos.

06

Buffers are insurance — price them like it

Strategic inventory, capacity reservations, and flexibility clauses all carry premiums. The rational question is whether the premium is less than exposure × probability. Sometimes it isn't, and accepting the risk is correct — but that should be a documented decision, not an accident of budget pressure.

07

Response speed is a designed property

Organisations that recover fast pre-decided who convenes, what authority they hold, and what the playbook's first ten moves are. In a shortage, the buyer who calls the supplier in hour one with volume authority beats the one who convenes a committee in week two. Speed comes from pre-authorisation, not heroics.

08

Resilience decays without exercise

Alternate sources drift out of qualification, contacts go stale, playbooks rot. Untested resilience is a hypothesis. Periodic exercises — a simulated supplier failure, a real volume shift to the second source — are what convert paper resilience into working capability.

Resilience Levers

Six families of intervention. None is universally right — each is bought with cost, complexity, or leverage, and matched to the exposure it addresses.

Multi-Sourcing & Qualification Depth+

The strongest structural lever: two or more genuinely qualified, tooled, volume-receiving suppliers on different failure paths (different geographies, different upstream dependencies). Variants: full dual-source (both live), warm alternate (qualified, small share), cold alternate (qualified once, dormant — weakest).

Costs: duplicated NRE/tooling, split volume leverage, double qualification maintenance. Fit: A-class strategic components where TTR > TTS and redesign is slow.

Strategic Buffers+

Inventory held explicitly against disruption (distinct from cycle/safety stock): sized from TTS-vs-TTR gaps on critical items. Options: buyer-held stock, supplier-held buffer under contract, or bonded/3PL stock at a neutral point. For electronics, die banking (holding wafers/die before packaging) buys long cover at lower cost and lower obsolescence exposure than finished parts.

Discipline: strategic buffer is ring-fenced — it is not available to cover forecast misses, or it will silently become safety stock and be gone when the event arrives.

Geographic & Logistics Diversification+

Reducing correlated exposure: nearshoring or regionalising supply for critical items, qualifying alternate ports and freight lanes, and avoiding single-country concentration for any component family. For NZ-based operations the logistics dimension is acute — long, thin inbound lanes mean freight disruption is a primary risk, not a footnote.

Honest trade: regional supply usually carries a unit-cost premium. Price it against the exposure it removes; apply it selectively to the critical path, not the whole portfolio.

Contractual Flexibility & Capacity Rights+

Resilience written into commercial terms: volume flexibility bands (e.g. ±25% at 8 weeks), capacity reservation fees, allocation priority clauses, disclosed sub-tier dependencies, and mandatory disruption notification within defined hours. In shortage conditions, contractual allocation priority — agreed when you had leverage — is worth more than relationship goodwill.

Negotiate when calm: none of these terms is obtainable mid-crisis. They are bought during normal sourcing events, priced as modest premiums, and exercised rarely.

Design for Resilience+

The upstream lever most procurement teams never pull: influencing engineering toward multi-sourced components, standard packages over sole-source exotics, approved alternates on the BOM at design time, and component commonality across products (which pools demand and simplifies buffering). A resilient BOM beats any amount of downstream heroics.

Mechanism: a sourcing-risk gate in the NPI process — every new sole-source component requires justification and a documented mitigation before design freeze.

Sensing & Early Warning+

Shortening detection time: supplier financial monitoring, PCN/EOL feeds for electronic components, geopolitical and weather watch on concentrated nodes, and lead-time trend tracking (a creeping quote lead time is often the first public signal of allocation). Detection days ahead of competitors translates directly into allocation position and broker-market cost.

Keep it proportionate: monitor the critical-path map, not the universe. A tight watch on 40 nodes beats a shallow feed on 4,000.

Six steps from unknown exposure to exercised capability. The sequence matters — levers chosen before mapping produce expensive protection in the wrong places.

Common Failure Modes

Resilience programmes fail through comfortable illusions — paper alternates, raided buffers, and maps that stop at Tier 1.

01

Paper dual sourcing

A second source qualified years ago, on an old revision, with no tooling and no volume, listed in the register as "mitigated." When the primary fails, the alternate's real lead time turns out to be nine months.

Define minimum conditions for "qualified alternate" — current revision, tooling status, refresh cadence, minimum volume share. Audit annually; downgrade anything that fails.
02

Mapping stops at Tier 1

Diversification counted by Tier 1 logos while all paths converge on one fab, one port, or one refiner two tiers up. The map says resilient; the structure says single point of failure.

For critical items, trace until paths genuinely diverge. Contractually require Tier 1s to disclose manufacturing sites and critical sub-tier dependencies.
03

Strategic buffers raided for forecast misses

Disruption stock quietly consumed to cover ordinary planning errors. The register still shows twelve weeks of cover; the shelf holds three.

Ring-fence strategic stock with separate ownership and release authority. Any draw triggers replenishment and a root-cause note.
04

Buying resilience everywhere

Blanket dual-sourcing mandates and across-the-board buffer increases spend the resilience budget on low-exposure items, leaving the genuinely dangerous nodes under-protected because the money ran out.

Fund from the ranked exposure register only. Resilience is targeted insurance, not a uniform coating.
05

No pre-authorised response authority

The disruption hits; the first week disappears into meetings about who may approve premium freight and whether the broker buy needs three quotes. Allocation position is lost in exactly the window it's decided.

Pre-authorise crisis spend limits and decision rights in the playbook, signed off by finance before any event. Speed is bought in advance.
06

Resilience as a one-off project

A post-crisis programme produces maps, registers, and a binder — then attention moves on. Three years later the map is fiction and the alternates are cold.

Assign standing ownership, a refresh calendar, and an annual exercise. Report programme health (map currency, alternate status, buffer integrity) to leadership yearly.
07

Ignoring the demand side of survival

TTS calculated on inventory alone, overlooking demand levers — product substitution, allocation to priority customers, feature variants that avoid the constrained part. Survival time is understated and buffers oversized.

Include demand flexibility in TTS: which products can flex, which customers are protected first, which BOM alternates exist. Agree these with product and sales before the event.
08

Learning nothing from the near miss

Disruptions that almost hurt — the allocation you escaped, the freight delay absorbed by luck — pass without analysis, wasting the cheapest learning available.

Run lightweight post-mortems on near misses, not just direct hits. Each one is a free stress test; feed findings straight into the register and playbook.

Sustainable & Ethical Procurement

Embedding environmental, social, and governance obligations into how the organisation buys — driven by regulation, customer flow-downs, and the supply chain's outsized share of most companies' total impact.

First Principles

Sustainable procurement is risk management and market access, not philanthropy. The obligations arrive through law and through customers — and they land on procurement because the supply chain is where most of the exposure lives.

01

Your supply chain is most of your footprint — and most of your risk

For a typical manufacturer, the large majority of carbon emissions (Scope 3) and nearly all modern-slavery exposure sit with suppliers, not in your own operations. Any credible ESG position therefore runs through procurement. This is why the obligations keep landing on your desk: you hold the commercial relationships where the exposure lives.

02

Obligations flow down — and you flow them down again

Large customers discharge their own legal and investor commitments by imposing requirements on suppliers: codes of conduct, emissions disclosure, audit rights, conflict-mineral declarations. You receive these as a supplier and must pass the relevant parts to your own supply base. Procurement is the transmission mechanism in both directions.

03

Risk-based effort, not uniform coverage

Auditing every supplier equally is impossible and pointless. Exposure concentrates by category (electronics assembly, raw material extraction, low-wage labour services), by geography, and by workforce type. Segment the supply base by inherent ESG risk and concentrate due diligence where the exposure actually is — the same portfolio logic as Kraljic, applied to a different risk axis.

04

Transparency beats false assurance

Regulators and sophisticated customers do not expect a clean supply chain; they expect you to know it, disclose honestly, and show improvement. A statement that says "we found risk indicators here and are remediating" is stronger — legally and reputationally — than one claiming zero issues, which nobody believes and any journalist can falsify.

05

Remediate before you terminate

Cutting a supplier at the first finding punishes disclosure, drives problems underground, and frequently harms the affected workers a second time. Leading practice is corrective action with deadlines, support, and verification — reserving exit for refusal to engage or severe, unremediated harm. Your leverage to improve conditions exists only while the commercial relationship does.

06

ESG criteria belong inside the sourcing decision, not beside it

If sustainability is a policy statement that never appears in supplier selection scoring, contract clauses, or QBR agendas, it is decoration. The test of a real programme: could an ESG factor change a sourcing outcome? If the answer is no, the programme is cosmetic.

07

Data quality is the practical constraint

Scope 3 reporting, materials declarations, and origin tracing all depend on data suppliers may not hold, may estimate poorly, or may be reluctant to share. Build capability progressively: spend-based estimates first, supplier-specific data for the concentrated categories, primary data only where materiality justifies the collection burden — on both sides.

08

Sustainability requirements are commercial terms — price them

Certifications, audits, data provision, and material substitutions cost suppliers real money, and that cost returns in price. Sequence and prioritise requirements, contribute to shared audit schemes where possible, and accept that some obligations are worth paying for. Pretending compliance is free just pushes suppliers toward box-ticking.

Where Exposure Concentrates

Electronics assembly and component manufacture (migrant labour, recruitment-fee bondage risk) · raw material extraction (3TG minerals, mica, cobalt) · low-wage onshore services — cleaning, security, logistics labour hire (often the highest domestic modern-slavery risk, and routinely overlooked because the spend is small) · packaging and timber products (deforestation, origin fraud).

Signals of Elevated Risk

Recruitment fees charged to workers · retained passports or documents · labour-hire layers obscuring the true employer · prices structurally below any plausible compliant cost base · refusal of site access or worker interviews · certificates that cannot be verified with the issuing body. Any of these moves a supplier up the due-diligence queue regardless of category.

The Assurance Ladder

Ascending cost and confidence: self-assessment questionnaire → desktop verification of certificates → shared/industry audit (RBA, SMETA) → independent site audit with worker interviews → ongoing worker-voice channels. Match the rung to inherent risk; reserve the expensive rungs for the exposure that warrants them.

Evidence Discipline

Every obligation family ultimately reduces to evidence on request: statements, declarations, audit reports, corrective-action records. Keep them centrally, mapped to supplier and contract, with expiry tracking. The organisation that can produce evidence in a day wins customer audits; the one that scrambles for weeks loses bids.

Common Failure Modes

The gap between stated policy and purchasing behaviour is where sustainable procurement programmes are judged — and where most fail.

01

Policy–practice schism

A public commitment to ethical sourcing while award decisions run on price alone, squeezing the very suppliers expected to fund compliance. Suppliers read the purchasing signal, not the policy document.

Put ESG criteria where money moves: selection scorecards, negotiation mandates, QBR agendas. If it can't change an outcome, it isn't a programme.
02

Questionnaire theatre

Hundreds of self-assessments collected, filed, and never verified or acted on. Coverage statistics look excellent; assurance value is near zero — and the paper trail proves you saw the risk indicators and did nothing.

Fewer, better: verify a sample, escalate red flags to real audits, and record what changed as a result of each finding.
03

Audit-and-run

Findings issued, relationship ended, problem exported to someone else's supply chain — often worsening conditions for the workers involved and teaching every other supplier to hide issues.

Default to remediation with deadlines and support; reserve exit for refusal or severe unremediated harm, and execute exits responsibly.
04

Uniform effort across the base

The same questionnaire to the sole-trader courier and the offshore CM — burden spread evenly, assurance concentrated nowhere. High-risk nodes get the same shallow look as trivial ones.

Risk-tier everything. Deep, verified diligence on the small high-risk tier; light touch elsewhere; documented rationale for the tiers.
05

Overlooking the domestic low-spend risk

Attention fixed on offshore manufacturing while cleaning, security, and labour-hire contracts onshore — classic modern-slavery territory — pass through on lowest price without a second look.

Include labour-intensive domestic services in the high-risk tier regardless of spend size. Price abnormally below compliant cost is itself a red flag.
06

Claiming beyond the evidence

Marketing statements — "slavery-free", "fully traceable", "carbon neutral supply chain" — that the data cannot support. Greenwashing exposure now carries regulatory teeth as well as reputational cost.

Legal-review public claims against held evidence. Disclose risk found and action taken; credibility compounds, overstatement detonates.
07

Scope 3 perfectionism

Waiting for perfect supplier-level emissions data before doing anything — years of methodology debate, no reduction levers pulled, while the concentrated categories sat visible in a spend-based estimate all along.

Start spend-based, refine the material categories with supplier data, act on the concentration you can already see. Estimate → act → improve the estimate.
08

Compliance without ownership

ESG treated as an annual statement produced by legal or comms, disconnected from category managers who never see the risk map and own no actions. The statement exists; the supply chain is unchanged.

Embed actions in category plans with named owners and dates. The statutory statement should summarise work that happened anyway — not constitute it.

Procurement Governance & Policy

The control architecture that makes procurement decisions authorised, defensible, and consistent — protecting the organisation and the individuals inside it.

First Principles

Governance is not bureaucracy for its own sake — it is the system that lets an organisation delegate spending authority to hundreds of people without losing control of the money or the ethics.

01

Governance protects the individual as much as the organisation

A documented process, a clear delegation, and a declared conflict are what stand between a procurement professional and an allegation they cannot disprove. Working inside the framework is personal protection — the audit trail is your alibi. People who see governance only as friction have never needed it to defend them.

02

Authority must be explicit, delegated, and bounded

Every commitment of company money should trace to a named authority level in a Delegation of Authority (DoA) matrix — who can approve what value, for what type of commitment, under what conditions. Ambiguity about who may approve is how unauthorised commitments happen in good faith.

03

Separation of duties is the load-bearing control

No single person should be able to create a supplier, raise a purchase order, receive the goods, and approve the invoice. The 3-way match (PO / receipt / invoice) and requisitioner-approver separation are the structural defences against both fraud and honest error. Every control simplification proposal should be tested against this principle first.

04

Policy earns compliance through usability

A policy that is proportionate, findable, and fast gets followed; one that adds weeks to simple purchases gets bypassed — and every bypass builds the maverick-spend channel that eventually carries something serious. The governance designer's job is making the compliant path the easiest path.

05

Thresholds should match risk, not habit

Approval levels, quote requirements, and tender thresholds are risk instruments: low-value purchases deserve near-zero friction; high-value and high-risk commitments deserve genuine scrutiny. Thresholds inherited from a smaller organisation a decade ago usually over-control the trivial and under-control the material.

06

Conflicts of interest are managed by declaration, not absence

In a market the size of New Zealand's, connections between buyers and suppliers are unavoidable. The control is not pretending they don't exist — it is declaring them, recording them, and removing the conflicted person from the specific decision. An undeclared minor conflict is more damaging than a declared major one.

07

Exceptions are governance too

Every framework needs a legitimate fast path — emergencies, sole sources, urgent operational need. The test of governance maturity is not zero exceptions; it is that exceptions are visible, justified, approved at the right level, and reviewed for patterns. An exception process that is easier than the standard process has just become the standard process.

08

The audit trail is the deliverable

Decisions age; memories fade; people leave. What remains is the record: the evaluation matrix, the approval chain, the declared conflicts, the exception justification. Governance that lives in conversations and cannot be reconstructed on paper does not exist for audit purposes — and audit purposes arrive eventually.

Governance Architecture

Five structural components. Together they answer: what are the rules, who may commit, how is power separated, how is integrity protected, and how do exceptions flow.

The Policy Hierarchy

Delegation of Authority (DoA)

The matrix mapping commitment type × value × role to approval requirements — purchase commitments, contract signature, variations, disposals, and exceptions each need their own line. Key design rules: authority attaches to the role, not the person; contract signature and PO approval are distinct authorities; aggregate/annual value governs, not instalment size — splitting a commitment to duck a threshold is itself a breach.

Separation of Duties (SoD)

The incompatible-pairs list: supplier creation vs. payment approval; requisition vs. approval; receipt vs. invoice approval; evaluation vs. sole approval of your own recommendation. In small teams where perfect separation is impossible, compensate with dual sign-off, rotation, and targeted audit sampling — and document the compensating control, not just the gap.

Ethics & Integrity Controls

Gifts and hospitality register with value thresholds and pre-approval above them; conflict-of-interest declarations refreshed annually and at each sourcing event; anti-bribery rules covering facilitation payments and third-party intermediaries; confidentiality of supplier pricing during live events; a protected route for raising concerns. These controls exist because procurement sits closest to the money — and to the people who want it.

Exception & Sole-Source Management

A defined route for departing from standard process: documented justification, approval one level above the normal authority, time or value bounds, and central logging. Sole-source justifications deserve particular rigour — "only supplier who can" claims should be tested, dated, and re-verified at renewal, because markets change while justifications fossilise.

The Coverage Metric That Matters

Spend under management — the share of addressable spend flowing through governed channels (contracts, catalogues, PO process) — is the single best summary indicator of governance health. Low SUM means the framework exists on paper while money moves around it. Track it, trend it, and treat every persistent bypass channel as a design flaw to fix, not a user failing to punish.

Six steps that keep governance alive — because a framework is not what the documents say, it is what the organisation actually does when the documents aren't being watched.

01

Define the policy hierarchy, DoA matrix, quote/tender thresholds, and SoD rules — calibrated to current organisation size and risk, not inherited habit. Design the low-value path to be nearly frictionless; spend the control budget on material and high-risk commitments.

Approval routing, threshold enforcement, SoD checks, and vendor-master controls belong in the ERP/P2P system where they execute automatically. A control that depends on everyone remembering the policy is a suggestion. System-enforced controls also generate their own audit trail for free.

Common Failure Modes

Governance fails in two directions — too little control invites loss and scandal; too much invites the workarounds that create the same exposure with better camouflage.

01

Friction-driven bypass

Controls so slow for routine purchases that the organisation industrialises workarounds — P-card splitting, invoice-without-PO, "urgent" everything. Maverick channels built for lunch orders eventually carry consulting contracts.

Measure cycle time on low-value purchases and drive it toward hours. Every persistent bypass is a design flaw; redesign the path rather than policing the users.
02

Threshold gaming tolerated

Commitments split into instalments that each duck under approval or tender thresholds — visible in the data, unaddressed in practice, until an auditor aggregates the pattern in an afternoon.

Define thresholds on aggregate annual value per supplier/need; run split-detection analytics; treat deliberate splitting as the policy breach it is.
03

Sole-source justifications that fossilise

"Only supplier who can do this" — written five years ago, never re-tested, while the market grew three capable alternatives. The exception has quietly become a permanent uncompeted channel.

Date-limit every sole-source approval; require market re-verification at renewal; report aging justifications to governance forums.
04

Conflicts undeclared because "everyone knows"

Small-market familiarity treated as a substitute for declaration — until a tender loss, a falling-out, or a journalist reframes the friendship as corruption and there is no record showing it was managed.

Normalise declaration as routine hygiene: annual refresh plus per-event confirmation, recorded centrally, conflicted parties visibly recused. Declared and managed is defensible; known and undocumented is not.
05

DoA drift after reorganisations

Roles renamed, teams merged, and the delegation matrix left describing an org chart that no longer exists — approvals flowing on habit and assumed authority.

Bind DoA review into every restructure and the annual cycle; reconcile system approval routes against the current matrix, not folklore.
06

Control theatre on trivial spend, silence on material risk

Three quotes demanded for a $400 purchase while a seven-figure renewal rolls over uncompeted — friction allocated by habit and visibility rather than exposure.

Map control effort against spend and risk annually; strip friction from the trivial and reinvest it in renewals, variations, and high-risk categories where loss actually lives.
07

Exception path becomes the main road

An "urgent operational need" route with lighter scrutiny that grows to carry a large share of spend — because it is faster, and speed always wins against unattended process design.

Track exception share and reasons monthly; cap tolerated volume; when a legitimate need pattern emerges, build it a proper fast lane with proportionate controls.
08

Governance owned by no one

Policies written at an ERP go-live, PDFs on an intranet, no owner, no review cycle, no relationship to what people actually do. The framework exists archaeologically.

Name an owner for the framework and each policy; publish the review calendar; retire what is dead. A smaller, living rulebook outperforms a comprehensive dead one.

The Four Groups

E — departure (EXW): buyer does everything. F — main carriage unpaid (FCA/FAS/FOB): buyer arranges shipping. C — main carriage paid (CFR/CIF/CPT/CIP): seller pays freight but risk transfers at origin — the trap group. D — arrival (DAP/DPU/DDP): seller carries risk to destination.

FOB/CIF are maritime-only terms — for containers use FCA/CIP instead. CIF insurance is minimum cover (Institute Clauses C) — often inadequate for electronics; specify better or insure yourself. Formulas & Calculations

The working arithmetic. Each formula answers one commercial question — the variables matter more than the algebra.

Economic Order Quantity
EOQ = √( 2 × D × S ÷ H )
D = annual demand (units) · S = cost per order · H = holding cost per unit per year. Minimises ordering + carrying cost. Compare against supplier MOQs to price what the MOQ is costing you.
Safety Stock
SS = Z × σd × √LT
Z = service factor (90% → 1.28 · 95% → 1.65 · 98% → 2.05 · 99% → 2.33) · σd = std dev of demand per period · LT = lead time in periods. Scales linearly with Z, with the square root of lead time.
Reorder Point
ROP = (d̄ × LT) + SS
d̄ = average demand per period · LT = lead time in periods · SS = safety stock. Trigger on stock position (on hand + on order − allocated), never on-hand alone.
Inventory Turns
Turns = COGS ÷ Average Inventory
Higher = capital working harder. Days of inventory (DIO) = 365 ÷ turns. Watch the trend, not the absolute — turns vary hugely by industry.
Cash Conversion Cycle
CCC = DIO + DSO − DPO
DIO = days inventory · DSO = days sales outstanding · DPO = days payables outstanding = (AP ÷ COGS) × 365. Procurement moves DPO (terms) and DIO (inventory policy).
Payment Terms → Working Capital
Cash impact = Daily spend × Δdays
Daily spend = annual spend ÷ 365. Extending a NZD $10M supplier from 30 to 45 days releases ≈ $411k cash. Value the release at your cost of capital; weigh against any early-payment discount offered.
Early Payment Discount — Annualised
Rate ≈ d ÷ (100−d) × 365 ÷ (T − t)
d = discount % · t = discount window · T = standard terms. "2/10 net 30" ≈ 37% annualised — usually worth taking if cash allows. Compare against your cost of capital.
Purchase Price Variance
PPV = (Std price − Actual price) × Qty
Positive = favourable vs. standard. Useful as a tracking signal; dangerous as a target — PPV can be gamed by inflating standards or buying bulk into inventory.
Savings — Cost Reduction
Saving = (Baseline − New price) × Volume
Baseline = last price paid (like-for-like spec, terms, incoterm). Only P&L-visible with volume actually purchased. State baseline type explicitly; get finance sign-off on methodology once, then reuse.
Cost Avoidance
Avoidance = (Quoted/indexed increase − Settled) × Vol
Value of an increase resisted or specification saving vs. credible counterfactual. Real, but invisible in the P&L — always report separately from reduction, never blended.
Should-Cost Gap
Gap % = (Quote − Model) ÷ Model × 100
The negotiation agenda in one number. Decompose by cost layer (material / labour / overhead / margin) to locate where the conversation should focus.
Landed Cost
Unit + freight + duty + insurance + handling
Duty is calculated on customs value which may include freight/insurance depending on jurisdiction and incoterm. Compare suppliers only on landed cost at the same incoterm — never mixed terms.
